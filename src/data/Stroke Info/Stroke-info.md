# What is Stroke?
A Stroke Happens When The Brain’s Blood Supply Is Suddenly Interrupted Or When Bleeding Occurs Inside The Brain. There Are Two Main Types Of Strokes. An Ischemic Stroke Occurs When A Blood Vessel Supplying The Brain Becomes Blocked. Without Blood Flow, The Brain Is Deprived Of Oxygen And Nutrients, And Brain Cells Can Begin To Die Within Minutes. A Hemorrhagic Stroke Happens When A Blood Vessel In The Brain Ruptures And Causes Bleeding. The Leaking Blood Creates Pressure Inside The Skull, Which Can Damage Surrounding Brain Tissue. Nearly 90% Of Strokes Are Ischemic, While The Remaining Cases Are Hemorrhagic. Strokes Are Also Categorized By The Specific Area Of The Brain Affected.

A Stroke Is A Serious Medical Emergency That Requires Immediate Attention. It Can Lead To Permanent Brain Injury, Long-Term Disability, Or Death. Symptoms Vary Depending On The Severity And Location Of The Stroke. Common Warning Signs Include Weakness Or Numbness On One Side Of The Body Or Face, Difficulty Speaking Or Understanding Speech, Sudden Vision Problems, Severe Headache, Dizziness, Or Loss Of Coordination.

If You Suspect That You Or Someone Else Is Having A Stroke, Call 9-1-1 Immediately. Do Not Attempt To Drive Yourself Or Have Someone Else Drive You To The Hospital. Emergency Medical Professionals Can Begin Treatment On The Way To The Hospital, Which Can Significantly Improve Outcomes. Quick Action Is Critical Because Brain Cells Are Lost Every Minute A Stroke Goes Untreated.
Once At The Hospital, A Specialized Medical Team Will Evaluate The Situation And Determine The Best Course Of Treatment, Which May Include Medication, Surgery, Or Other Medical Procedures. Recovery Depends On The Severity Of The Stroke And How Quickly Treatment Was Provided. Rehabilitation Therapy Can Help Individuals Regain Skills And Return To Daily Activities As Much As Possible.

# Symptoms of Stroke
Stroke symptoms usually appear suddenly, but in some cases they can develop gradually over several hours or days. This may occur when a transient ischemic attack (TIA), sometimes called a “mini-stroke,” progresses into a full stroke. A TIA happens when blood flow to part of the brain is temporarily blocked but restores before permanent damage occurs.
The specific warning signs depend on the type of stroke and the area of the brain involved. Common symptoms of a TIA or stroke include:

- Sudden difficulty speaking, understanding speech, or sudden confusion
- Sudden weakness or numbness, particularly on one side of the body
- A sudden, intense headache with no clear cause
- Sudden vision problems in one or both eyes
- Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, or problems with balance and coordination

## A helpful way to recognize a possible stroke is by remembering the FAST acronym:

- **Face**: Ask the person to smile. Does one side of the face appear uneven or droop?
- **Arms**: Ask the person to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward or seem weak?
- **Speech**: Ask the person to repeat a simple sentence. Is their speech slurred, unclear, or unusual?
- **Time**: If you notice any of these warning signs, call 9-1-1 immediately. Prompt medical care can greatly improve the chances of recovery.

If you believe someone may be experiencing a TIA or stroke, do not drive them to the hospital yourself. Call emergency services right away. Paramedics can begin treatment during transport, which can save brain function and lives. When it comes to stroke, acting quickly makes all the difference.

# Complications
A stroke can lead to serious and lasting effects, including brain damage, long-term disability, or even death. The severity of a stroke depends on how much brain tissue was affected. When a stroke causes significant damage, doctors may refer to it as a **massive stroke**, which can bring more serious complications.
After a stroke, patients may face a range of possible complications:

**1. Blood Clots:**
Limited movement after a stroke increases the risk of blood clots forming in the deep veins of the legs. These clots can sometimes travel to the lungs, causing serious complications. Doctors may prevent clots with medications or special devices that apply pressure to the legs to keep blood flowing.

**2. Loss of Bladder or Bowel Control:**
Strokes can affect the muscles controlling urination and bowel movements. A temporary urinary catheter may be needed until bladder control returns, though its use can increase the risk of infections. Bowel problems such as incontinence or constipation may also occur.

**3. Muscle Weakness and Mobility Problems:**
Stroke can cause weakness, stiffness, or muscle spasms, often on one side of the body. These issues can make walking, standing, or maintaining balance difficult and increase the risk of falls. Physical therapy is important to regain strength and coordination.

**4. Loss of Bone Density:**
Limited movement can lead to weaker bones, particularly on the side of the body affected by the stroke. Rehabilitation exercises and monitoring for osteoporosis can help prevent bone loss.

**5. Cognitive and Speech Issues:**
Stroke can affect thinking, memory, attention, and decision-making. Some people may develop dementia over time. Communication can also be impacted if the stroke affects the speech muscles, making it harder to talk or understand language.

**6. Seizures:**
Seizures can occur in the weeks following a stroke. Doctors may prescribe medication to reduce or control these seizures.

**7. Brain Swelling:**
After a stroke, fluid can accumulate inside or around the brain, increasing pressure and causing further damage. In severe cases, surgery may be required to remove fluid or relieve pressure.

**8. Sensory Changes:**
Stroke may affect vision, hearing, or the sense of touch. Patients may also experience reduced ability to sense temperature or pain, which can impact daily activities like cooking, reading, or dressing.

**9. Swallowing Difficulties and Pneumonia:**
If the stroke affects swallowing muscles, eating and drinking may become difficult, increasing the risk of food or liquids entering the lungs. This can lead to pneumonia.
Recovery and rehabilitation after a stroke are highly individual. Early medical care, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy can significantly improve outcomes and help reduce these complications.

# Understanding Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)
**Venous thromboembolism (VTE)** is a condition in which a blood clot forms in a vein. It includes two main types: **deep vein thrombosis (DVT)** and **pulmonary embolism (PE)**.

## Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) 
A clot forms in a deep vein, usually in the lower leg, thigh, or pelvis. DVT can also occur in the arms, especially if a large intravenous line is present. Symptoms may include swelling, redness, warmth, or pain in the affected area.

## Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
This occurs when a clot breaks free and travels to the lungs, blocking blood flow. PE can cause sudden chest pain, shortness of breath, rapid heartbeat, or fainting. Large clots can be life-threatening.
VTE is relatively common. In the United States, up to 600,000 VTE events happen each year.

## Risk Factors for VTE
The likelihood of developing VTE increases in situations where blood flow slows, veins are injured, or the body’s clotting system is more active. Common risk factors include:

- Major surgery or serious injury
- Prolonged periods of immobility, such as bed rest or long travel
- Severe infection or inflammation
Even without obvious symptoms, VTE can occur, making early detection important.

## Prevention and Treatment
If you are at risk for VTE, your healthcare provider may recommend:

- Medications to prevent clotting
- Compression devices to improve blood flow in the legs
- Lifestyle strategies, such as walking or moving around after surgery

For those diagnosed with VTE, treatment typically involves **anticoagulant medications** to prevent existing clots from growing and new clots from forming. Untreated VTE can reduce blood and oxygen flow, causing tissue or organ damage. In the case of a pulmonary embolism, this can be life-threatening. Prompt treatment is essential for reducing complications and improving outcomes.

# Understanding Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

## What is DVT?
Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is the most common form of venous thromboembolism (VTE). It happens when a blood clot forms in a deep vein, usually in the lower leg, thigh, or pelvis. Blood clots can also develop in the arms, particularly when large IV lines are present.
Symptoms of DVT
DVT can cause noticeable changes in the affected area, including:

- Swelling in one leg
- Pain or tenderness
- Cramping, aching, or a feeling of warmth
- Red or discolored skin

Some people may have DVT without obvious symptoms, which makes awareness of risk factors important.

## How DVT is Diagnosed
Healthcare providers use a combination of **medical history**, **physical exam**, and **tests** to diagnose DVT:

- D-dimer test: Measures a substance released when a blood clot breaks down. High levels may indicate a clot.
- Compression ultrasound: Uses sound waves to create images of blood flow in the veins and check if veins are stiff or compressed.
- Magnetic resonance venography (MRV): Uses a special magnet and IV dye to image veins, usually when ultrasound results are unclear.

## Causes and Risk Factors
DVT can develop when:

- Blood flow slows in the deep veins
- The lining of a vein is damaged
- The blood itself clots more easily

**Common risk factors include**

- Age: Risk increases with age, though DVT can happen at any age
- Family history: Inherited genes may increase clotting tendency
- Immobility: Long flights, bed rest, or recovery after surgery raise risk
- Medical conditions: Blood clotting disorders, autoimmune diseases, heart problems, or infections.
- Sex and hormones: Women of childbearing age, especially those who are pregnant or taking hormonal therapy, have a higher risk; after menopause, women’s risk decreases

## Treatment for DVT
Most DVT cases can be managed at home with medication, though severe cases may require hospitalization. Common treatments include:
- Blood thinners (anticoagulants): Prevent clots from growing and reduce the risk of pulmonary embolism
- Alternative treatments: For those who cannot take blood thinners, other medications or procedures may be recommended

## Recovery and Prevention
After a DVT, it’s important to focus on recovery and preventing complications:
- Watch for post-thrombotic syndrome: Swelling, pain, or itchiness in the affected limb should be reported to your healthcare provider
- Prevent recurrence: Follow your medication plan, attend regular checkups, and discuss prevention strategies with your provider
- Adopt a healthy lifestyle: Include heart-healthy foods, regular physical activity, a healthy weight, and quitting smoking
- Support mental health: Anxiety or stress is common after a clot. Seek help from healthcare professionals if needed

## How DVT is Treated?
Most cases of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) can be managed at home with medications, but severe clots may require hospitalization for closer monitoring and treatment.
Blood-thinning medications (anticoagulants) are the most common treatment. They help prevent existing clots from growing and reduce the risk of a clot traveling to the lungs, which can cause a life-threatening pulmonary embolism. If you cannot take blood thinners, your healthcare provider may suggest alternative medicines or procedures to manage the clot.

## Recovery and Ongoing Care
Recovering from DVT involves more than just treating the clot. Your healthcare provider will guide you on how to stay healthy and prevent complications:
- Watch for post-thrombotic syndrome: This condition can cause swelling, pain, or itchiness in the affected leg. Report any symptoms to your provider.
- Prevent future clots: Take medications exactly as prescribed, attend regular checkups, and discuss strategies to reduce your risk.
- Adopt a healthy lifestyle: Include nutritious foods, regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, and avoiding smoking.
- Support your mental health: Experiencing anxiety, stress, or fear after a blood clot is common. Seek help from your healthcare provider if needed.
With proper treatment, lifestyle adjustments, and ongoing monitoring, most people can manage DVT effectively and reduce the risk of serious complications.


# Understanding Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

## What is Pulmonary Embolism?
Pulmonary embolism (PE) is a type of venous thromboembolism (VTE) that occurs when a blood clot breaks free and travels to the lungs. Most pulmonary embolisms come from blood clots in the deep veins of the legs, known as deep vein thrombosis (DVT). When the clot blocks blood flow in the lungs, it can be life-threatening.

## Symptoms of Pulmonary Embolism
Some people with PE may have no symptoms, while others experience signs that appear quickly or develop gradually over days to weeks. Common symptoms include:
- Shortness of breath
- Chest pain, especially with deep breaths
- Rapid breathing
- Increased heart rate
Less common symptoms can include:
- Coughing, sometimes with blood
- Anxiety or a sense of dread
- Lightheadedness or fainting
- Sweating

## How Pulmonary Embolism is Diagnosed
Healthcare providers use a combination of medical history, physical exams, and tests to diagnose PE:
- CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA): The main test for PE, this imaging scan shows blood clots in the lungs.
- Blood tests (D-dimer): Measures substances released when clots break down. High levels may indicate DVT or PE.
- Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan: Checks air flow and blood flow in the lungs to detect blockages.
- Pulmonary angiography: A specialized X-ray test that visualizes blood flow in the lungs; usually reserved for unclear cases.
- Other imaging or heart and lung function tests: Used if previous tests are inconclusive.

## Causes of Pulmonary Embolism
PE usually occurs when a clot from a deep vein (DVT) travels to the lungs. Clots can form due to:
- Vein injury from surgery or trauma
- Inflammation caused by infection or illness
- Reduced blood flow from immobility
- Rarely, clots can form directly in small blood vessels of the lungs

## Treatment for Pulmonary Embolism
Many people with PE can be treated with medications at home, but severe cases may require hospitalization. Treatments include:
- Blood-thinning medicines (anticoagulants): Prevent existing clots from growing and reduce the risk of new clots
- Alternative treatments: For patients who cannot take blood thinners, other procedures or medicines may be used
Without treatment, PE can lead to heart attack, shock, stroke, or death, making prompt care critical.

## Recovery and Ongoing Care
After a pulmonary embolism, recovery involves reducing the risk of future clots and managing possible complications:
- Watch for pulmonary hypertension: This is high blood pressure in the lungs’ blood vessels, which can lead to heart failure. Symptoms include shortness of breath (especially with exercise), swelling, coughing up blood, fainting, or feeling a racing heartbeat. Severe cases may require surgery to remove remaining clots.
- Prevent repeat DVT or PE: Take medications as prescribed, attend regular checkups, and discuss risk-reduction strategies with your healthcare provider.
- Adopt a heart-healthy lifestyle: Eat nutritious foods, stay physically active, maintain a healthy weight, and avoid smoking.
- Support your mental health: Anxiety, stress, or fear are common after a clot. Seek help from your healthcare provider if needed.

# Diagnosis (Stroke)

## Diagnosing a Stroke
If a stroke is suspected, your healthcare provider will evaluate your symptoms, medical history, physical condition, and test results. The goal is to determine the type of stroke, its cause, the part of the brain affected, and whether bleeding has occurred. If a transient ischemic attack (TIA), or “mini-stroke,” is suspected, your provider will investigate the cause to prevent future strokes.

### Medical History and Physical Exam
Your doctor will ask about:
- Previous strokes or TIAs in you or your family
- Current symptoms and when they began
- Risk factors such as high blood pressure, diabetes, heart disease, smoking, or high cholesterol

## During a physical exam, your provider will check for:
- Confusion or trouble thinking clearly
- Coordination and balance issues
- Weakness or numbness in the face, arms, or legs
- Difficulty speaking, understanding speech, or seeing clearly

The exam helps your doctor determine the severity of the stroke and plan appropriate treatment.
Your provider may also check for carotid artery disease, a common cause of ischemic stroke. Using a stethoscope, they listen for a “bruit,” a whooshing sound that may indicate plaque buildup reducing blood flow in the carotid arteries.

### Diagnostic Tests
Several tests are used to confirm a stroke and identify its type:

## Imaging Tests
- Computed Tomography (CT): Uses X-rays to create detailed brain images. A CT scan can quickly show bleeding or brain cell damage after a suspected stroke.
- Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): Uses magnets and radio waves to detect changes in brain tissue. An MRI may be done in addition to or instead of a CT scan.
- Other imaging: In some cases, digital subtraction angiography (DSA) or positron emission tomography (PET) may be used to examine blood vessels, detect aneurysms, or identify arteriovenous malformations (tangled vessels) in the brain.

## Blood Tests
- Blood work helps check:
- Red blood cell and platelet levels
- Blood sugar (glucose)
- Clotting ability
- Muscle damage
These tests also help determine whether certain medications are safe to use.

## Heart Tests
- Electrocardiogram (EKG): Detects heart problems that may have caused the stroke, such as atrial fibrillation or a previous heart attack.

## Other Tests
- Lumbar puncture (spinal tap): If imaging does not show bleeding but a hemorrhagic stroke is suspected, fluid is collected from around the spine and tested for substances released from damaged blood cells.
By combining medical history, physical examination, and diagnostic tests, your healthcare provider can identify the cause and location of the stroke, determine its severity, and develop a treatment plan tailored to your needs.

## Causes and Risk Factors of Stroke
A stroke happens when blood flow to the brain is blocked **(ischemic stroke)** or when a blood vessel in the brain suddenly bleeds **(hemorrhagic stroke)**. Many factors can increase your risk of stroke. Some of these risk factors can be changed to help prevent a first stroke or a future stroke.
