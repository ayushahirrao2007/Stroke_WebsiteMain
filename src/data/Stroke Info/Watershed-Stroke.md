# Watershed Stroke

**Definition:** A watershed stroke is an ischemic stroke that occurs in the border zones (watershed areas) between the territories supplied by major cerebral arteries, typically due to reduced blood flow or severe drops in blood pressure.

## Abstract

Watershed strokes, also known as border zone infarcts, are a type of ischemic stroke that occur in regions of the brain located between the territories supplied by major cerebral arteries. These areas are particularly vulnerable due to their relatively low blood supply.

The development of watershed infarcts is primarily associated with reduced cerebral perfusion and, in some cases, embolic events originating from unstable atherosclerotic plaques. However, determining the exact mechanism in individual cases can be challenging, as both hemodynamic compromise and embolism may contribute simultaneously.

Advancements in neuroimaging have significantly improved the identification and characterization of these strokes, although their underlying pathophysiology remains complex and not fully understood. This review emphasizes the importance of imaging in diagnosis and highlights the need to correlate clinical findings with radiological features for effective management.

Early recognition and timely evaluation are essential for identifying the cause and initiating appropriate treatment strategies, ultimately improving patient outcomes.
**Keywords:** Ischemic stroke, hypoperfusion, carotid stenosis, watershed infarct

# Introduction
Watershed infarcts represent ischemic injury occurring at the junction of two arterial territories in the brain. These regions lie at the farthest ends of arterial supply, making them especially susceptible to reduced blood flow.

Two main types of watershed infarcts are recognized:

- **Cortical** (external) watershed infarcts, occurring at the boundaries between major cerebral arteries on the brain surface 
- **Internal** (deep) watershed infarcts, located within the white matter between deep and superficial arterial systems 

Although these strokes are often not immediately life-threatening, they are more common than previously assumed, particularly in individuals with significant narrowing of the internal carotid artery. Their clinical presentation can be subtle, progressive, or fluctuating, which may lead to underdiagnosis.

Symptoms may develop gradually over hours or days and can sometimes include focal neurological deficits or even seizure activity. Because of this variable and often mild presentation, diagnosing watershed infarcts can be challenging in clinical practice.

The exact mechanism behind these strokes is still debated. Reduced cerebral blood flow (hypoperfusion) and embolic phenomena are both considered key contributors. In many cases, both mechanisms may coexist.

Modern imaging techniques, especially diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging, play a crucial role in detecting these infarcts early and accurately. Given their clinical significance and diagnostic complexity, prompt identification and evaluation are essential to guide appropriate management and prevent further neurological damage.

# Materials and Methods
This review focuses on improving the understanding and recognition of watershed strokes, which are often underdiagnosed in clinical practice. A comprehensive literature search was conducted using a major biomedical database to identify relevant studies.

Keywords such as “watershed stroke,” “border zone infarct,” “carotid artery stenosis,” and “artery-to-artery embolism” were used to retrieve appropriate articles. No strict limitations were applied regarding study design, geographical location, or publication period to ensure a broad and inclusive review of available evidence.

Only articles published in English and available up to early 2023 were considered. In addition to database searches, references from selected studies were carefully reviewed to identify further relevant sources. Expert knowledge was also used to include important supporting literature.

Radiological examples referenced in this review were obtained from institutional clinical databases, supporting the discussion of imaging findings related to watershed infarcts.

## Anatomical Vascularization of the Brain
The blood supply of the brain is divided into two main systems: the anterior circulation and the posterior circulation. These systems arise from the internal carotid arteries and vertebral arteries, respectively.

The internal carotid artery originates from the common carotid artery and enters the skull through a canal in the temporal bone. It gives rise to several important branches, including the ophthalmic artery, posterior communicating artery, and anterior choroidal artery, before dividing into two major terminal branches: the anterior cerebral artery and the middle cerebral artery.

The anterior cerebral artery primarily supplies the medial surfaces of the frontal and parietal lobes, as well as portions of deeper brain structures. The middle cerebral artery supplies the lateral aspects of the cerebral hemispheres and also gives off deep branches that supply structures such as the basal ganglia and internal capsule.

The posterior circulation arises from the vertebral arteries, which merge to form the basilar artery. This system supplies the brainstem, cerebellum, and posterior regions of the brain. The posterior cerebral artery, a terminal branch of the basilar artery, is responsible for supplying the occipital lobe and parts of the temporal lobe.

## Clinical Significance of Vascular Variations
The occurrence and distribution of watershed strokes are closely related to variations in cerebral vascular anatomy. The circle of Willis plays a key role in maintaining collateral blood flow; however, anatomical variations can limit this compensatory mechanism.

For example, underdeveloped communicating arteries may reduce the brain’s ability to adapt to reduced blood flow, increasing the risk of ischemia. In some individuals, variations such as an abnormal origin of arteries or differences in vessel size may lead to unusual stroke patterns.

Certain anatomical configurations, such as a fetal-type posterior cerebral artery or abnormalities in segments of the anterior cerebral artery, can alter the typical distribution of blood flow and influence stroke presentation.

Additionally, variations in arterial branching patterns may lead to differences between clinical symptoms and imaging findings. Recognizing these variations is essential for accurate diagnosis and appropriate interpretation of imaging studies.

# Anatomy of Border Zone Infarcts
Watershed infarcts are classified based on their anatomical location into cortical (external), internal (deep), and less commonly, cerebellar types. Each type has distinct mechanisms, imaging features, and clinical implications.

## External (Cortical) Watershed Infarcts
Cortical watershed infarcts occur at the junction between the territories of major cerebral arteries, most commonly between the anterior and middle cerebral arteries, and between the middle and posterior cerebral arteries. These infarcts are often associated with small emboli, sometimes combined with reduced cerebral blood flow.

On imaging, they typically appear as wedge-shaped or triangular lesions involving the cortex and adjacent subcortical regions. These lesions are commonly located in the frontal and parieto-occipital regions, depending on the arterial territories involved.
From a pathological perspective, these infarcts result from insufficient oxygen and glucose delivery to neurons, leading to selective neuronal damage. This type of injury may also be seen in conditions such as global hypoxia or severe systemic hypotension.

Cortical watershed infarcts tend to have a relatively mild or subtle clinical presentation and are often associated with a favourable prognosis. They show a characteristic distribution pattern, with lesions frequently occurring in specific anterior and posterior regions while sparing the medial cortical areas.

## Internal (Deep) Watershed Infarcts
Internal watershed infarcts occur in the deep white matter, particularly in areas between the superficial cortical arteries and the deep perforating vessels. These regions include the corona radiata and centrum semiovale.

The primary mechanism behind these infarcts is reduced cerebral perfusion, often due to severe narrowing or occlusion of major arteries. Unlike cortical watershed infarcts, embolic factors may play a secondary role in some cases.

On imaging, these infarcts often appear as multiple small lesions arranged in a linear pattern parallel to the lateral ventricles. In some cases, these lesions merge to form continuous bands, producing a characteristic “string of pearls” appearance.
Internal watershed infarcts are generally associated with more severe hemodynamic compromise and carry a higher risk of poor outcomes. They are linked to increased morbidity, a greater likelihood of neurological deterioration, and a higher risk of recurrent stroke.

Different imaging patterns may reflect different underlying mechanisms. For example, scattered lesions may indicate reduced blood flow, while more confluent patterns may suggest a combination of hypoperfusion and embolic processes.

## Cerebellar Watershed Infarcts
Cerebellar watershed infarcts are relatively uncommon and typically smaller in size. They occur at the borders of arterial territories supplying the cerebellum, including branches of the anterior inferior, superior, and posterior inferior cerebellar arteries.

These infarcts are usually associated with reduced blood flow or embolic events originating from cardiac or large arterial sources. In some cases, arterial dissection within the vertebrobasilar system may also contribute.

Due to their rarity and variable presentation, cerebellar watershed infarcts are less frequently recognized in clinical practice. Their overall incidence remains unclear.

# Pathophysiology
Although the anatomical and imaging characteristics of watershed infarcts are well established, the exact mechanisms responsible for their development remain complex and not fully defined. In most cases, these strokes arise from a combination of reduced cerebral blood flow and embolic events.

A key mechanism is cerebral hypoperfusion, where there is insufficient blood supply to the brain. This may occur due to systemic conditions such as prolonged low blood pressure, cardiac arrest, or significant blood loss. When blood flow is globally reduced, the border zone regions—being the most distal areas of arterial supply—are particularly vulnerable. In such cases, infarcts may develop on both sides of the brain.

Several factors can trigger or worsen hypoperfusion, including sudden changes in posture, dehydration, anemia, excessive blood loss, and the use of certain medications such as antihypertensive agents. Individuals with impaired autonomic regulation, such as those with long-standing diabetes or neurodegenerative disorders, are especially susceptible to these changes.

Another important contributing factor is large vessel disease, particularly narrowing or blockage of the internal carotid artery. Significant arterial stenosis reduces the amount of blood reaching distal brain regions, making watershed areas more prone to ischemia. Even when compensatory pathways such as the circle of Willis are present, they may not provide adequate collateral circulation in all individuals.

In addition to hypoperfusion, embolism also plays a role. Small fragments from unstable atherosclerotic plaques or other sources can travel distally and lodge in smaller vessels, further compromising blood flow in already vulnerable regions.
Other less common causes include arterial dissection and certain vascular disorders that affect arterial structure and function. These conditions can further reduce blood supply or promote clot formation.

Research has shown differences in the underlying mechanisms of various types of watershed infarcts. Deep (internal) watershed infarcts are more strongly associated with reduced blood flow due to arterial narrowing or occlusion. In contrast, superficial (cortical) watershed infarcts are more commonly linked to embolic events.

Clinical outcomes also vary accordingly. Deep watershed infarcts are often associated with a higher risk of early neurological deterioration and poorer recovery, whereas cortical watershed infarcts tend to have a relatively better prognosis.

Overall, watershed strokes result from a complex interaction between hemodynamic compromise and embolic processes, with the relative contribution of each varying between patients and infarct types.

# Clinical Aspects
The clinical presentation of watershed strokes can vary widely depending on the affected brain region, but symptoms generally reflect impaired blood flow in vulnerable border zone areas. Common features include weakness or paralysis on one side of the body, difficulty speaking, facial asymmetry, visual disturbances, and abnormal eye movements.

In some cases, patients may experience fainting or near-fainting episodes at the onset of symptoms. This supports the role of reduced cerebral perfusion as a contributing factor. Watershed strokes are more frequently observed in older individuals, particularly those with conditions that affect blood pressure regulation, such as postural hypotension, cardiac rhythm abnormalities, or excessive use of blood pressure–lowering medications.

A characteristic presentation includes gradually worsening or fluctuating weakness, especially in the upper limb. These symptoms may appear intermittently and can progress over time. Seizures are reported more commonly in watershed strokes compared to other types of ischemic stroke, particularly in cortical involvement.

## Region-Specific Clinical Features

- **Cortical Border Zone (MCA–PCA regions):**
Infarcts in these areas may affect the parietal and temporal lobes, leading to visual disturbances such as loss of vision in one half of the visual field. Higher cortical dysfunction may also occur, including impaired spatial awareness, difficulty with coordinated eye movements, and problems with object recognition. Language disturbances may present as impaired comprehension with preserved speech fluency and repetition. 

- **Cognitive Effects:**
Repeated small infarcts in cortical watershed regions can result in gradual decline in cognitive abilities, particularly affecting memory and visuospatial skills. 
- **Deep (Subcortical) Watershed Infarcts:**
These may resemble lacunar stroke syndromes, presenting with motor or sensory deficits without prominent cortical signs. 
- **Cerebellar Involvement:**
When watershed infarcts occur in the cerebellum, symptoms are often nonspecific and may include dizziness, imbalance, or coordination difficulties. 
- **Brainstem Involvement:**
In rare cases, infarcts affecting border zones in the brainstem can result in severe neurological impairment, including reduced consciousness and vital function disturbances.

## Characteristic Clinical Syndrome

- **“Man-in-the-Barrel” Syndrome:**
Bilateral watershed infarcts between the anterior and middle cerebral artery territories can lead to weakness predominantly affecting the proximal muscles of both upper limbs, while lower limb strength is relatively preserved. This distinctive presentation results from symmetrical damage to motor pathways supplying the upper extremities. 

This syndrome must be differentiated from other conditions such as cervical spinal cord lesions, bilateral nerve injuries, or peripheral neuropathies.

# Stages of Hemodynamic Impairment
Hemodynamic impairment in watershed strokes can be understood in two progressive stages, based on the severity of reduced cerebral perfusion.

**Stage I: Compensated Hemodynamic State**
In the initial stage, a decrease in cerebral perfusion pressure triggers compensatory mechanisms within the brain. The small resistance vessels dilate to maintain adequate blood flow to brain tissue.

During this stage:
- Cerebral blood flow is relatively preserved 
- Cerebral blood volume increases due to vasodilation 
- Mean transit time of blood through the brain is prolonged 

These changes can be detected using advanced imaging techniques such as perfusion CT, MRI, and functional imaging modalities. This stage reflects the brain’s ability to adapt to reduced blood supply and prevent ischemic damage.

**Stage II: Decompensated Hemodynamic State**
In the second stage, compensatory mechanisms fail due to further reduction in perfusion pressure. As a result, cerebral blood flow decreases significantly.
To maintain metabolic function, brain tissue compensates by increasing oxygen extraction from the available blood supply. This leads to a state known as “misery perfusion,” characterized by:

- Reduced cerebral blood flow 
- Increased oxygen extraction fraction 
- High risk of tissue ischemia and infarction 

This stage indicates a critical level of hemodynamic compromise and is strongly associated with a higher risk of stroke and poor clinical outcomes.

# Imaging Diagnosis
Accurate diagnosis of watershed strokes relies on a combination of imaging techniques, each with specific advantages and limitations.

**Computed Tomography (CT Scan)**
Non-contrast CT is typically the first imaging modality used in acute stroke evaluation due to its speed and widespread availability. Its primary role is to exclude intracranial hemorrhage.

However, early ischemic changes may be subtle, especially in the hyperacute phase. Typical early findings include:
- Reduced density in affected brain tissue 
- Loss of distinction between gray and white matter 
- Early involvement of vulnerable regions such as the insular cortex 

As the condition progresses, more obvious changes such as brain swelling and clearer hypodensity may develop. In some cases, lesions may temporarily become less visible during the subacute phase, a phenomenon known as the “fogging effect.”

**CT Angiography (CTA)**
CT angiography provides detailed visualization of blood vessels in the head and neck. It is useful for:
- Detecting arterial narrowing or occlusion 
- Evaluating atherosclerotic plaques 
- Assessing the severity of carotid artery stenosis 

It is less invasive than conventional angiography but involves radiation exposure and contrast administration.

**Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)**
MRI is more sensitive than CT in detecting early ischemic changes. Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) can identify infarcts within minutes of onset, making it highly valuable in acute settings.
Other MRI sequences help characterize the stage of infarction:

- Early signal changes appear rapidly on DWI 
- T2 and FLAIR sequences detect evolving lesions 
- T1 changes appear later in the disease process 
MRI is particularly useful for identifying small or subtle watershed infarcts.

**Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA)**
MRA allows visualization of cerebral blood vessels with or without contrast. A commonly used technique is time-of-flight imaging, which provides good spatial resolution in a relatively short time.
Advantages include:
- Noninvasive assessment of vascular structure 
- Useful in evaluating blood flow patterns 

Limitations include reduced sensitivity in slow-flow vessels and susceptibility to imaging artifacts.

**Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA)**
DSA remains the gold standard for detailed vascular imaging. It provides high-resolution visualization of blood vessels and is particularly useful for planning interventional procedures.

However, it is invasive and carries a small risk of complications. It is mainly reserved for:
- Complex diagnostic cases 
- Interventional procedures such as stenting or thrombectomy

**Doppler Ultrasound**
Doppler ultrasound is a noninvasive and widely used method for evaluating carotid arteries. It helps:
- Detect arterial narrowing or blockage 
- Assess plaque characteristics 
- Evaluate blood flow dynamics 

It is often used as an initial screening tool, followed by more advanced imaging if significant abnormalities are detected.

# Differential Diagnosis
Watershed (border zone) infarcts must be differentiated from several neurological conditions that may present with similar clinical or imaging features. Accurate distinction is essential for appropriate management.

## Lacunar Infarcts
The most important differential diagnosis is lacunar stroke. These are small, deep infarcts caused by occlusion of penetrating arteries supplying structures such as the basal ganglia, thalamus, and brainstem.

Unlike watershed infarcts, lacunar lesions:
- Are typically small in size 
- Occur in deep brain regions 
- Do not follow the characteristic border zone distribution 
- Their random distribution helps distinguish them from the more patterned location of watershed infarcts.

**Multiple Embolic Infarcts**
Multiple embolic strokes may sometimes mimic watershed infarcts on imaging. However, embolic infarcts are usually:
- Scattered across multiple vascular territories 
- Often bilateral 
- Not confined to border zone regions 
- Their widespread distribution helps differentiate them from watershed patterns.

**Cerebral Small Vessel Diseases**
Certain inherited or acquired small vessel disorders can resemble watershed infarcts, including:
- CADASIL 
- CARASIL 
- MELAS 
- Fabry disease 

These conditions typically cause diffuse white matter changes and recurrent strokes, often with a genetic or metabolic basis. Clinical history and imaging patterns help in differentiation.

**Demyelinating Disorders**
Demyelinating diseases such as Multiple Sclerosis can produce neurological symptoms similar to watershed strokes, including weakness, speech difficulty, and coordination problems.

However, MRI findings differ:
- Lesions are commonly located near the ventricles or cortex 
- They often follow characteristic patterns along white matter tracts 
- Plaques are oriented perpendicular to the ventricles 

These imaging features help distinguish demyelinating conditions from ischemic infarcts.

# Management
The management of watershed stroke follows general principles of ischemic stroke care, with additional focus on restoring cerebral perfusion and addressing underlying vascular causes such as carotid artery disease.

## Acute Management

In the early phase, rapid assessment and timely intervention are critical:
- **Intravenous thrombolysis:**
Administration of alteplase is recommended within 4.5 hours of symptom onset in eligible patients. This helps restore blood flow and reduce infarct size. 
- **Mechanical thrombectomy:**
Patients with large vessel occlusion in the anterior circulation may benefit from endovascular treatment: 
    - Within 6 hours of symptom onset: strongly recommended 
    - Between 6 to 16 hours: recommended in selected patients based on imaging criteria 
    - Between 16 to 24 hours: may be considered in carefully selected cases 

Although watershed strokes are often related to hypoperfusion rather than large vessel occlusion, these interventions are important when an occlusive component is present.

## Management of Carotid Artery Disease
Significant carotid artery stenosis is a major contributor to watershed infarcts and requires targeted management:
- **Severe stenosis (70–99%):**
Carotid endarterectomy is strongly recommended if surgical risk is acceptable. 
- **Moderate stenosis (50–69%):**
Surgical intervention may be considered based on clinical and imaging findings. 
- **Mild stenosis (<50%):**
Surgical or interventional procedures are generally not indicated. 
- **Carotid artery stenting:**
May be used as an alternative in selected patients, especially when surgery is not suitable. 

All patients with carotid disease should receive intensive medical therapy, including antiplatelet agents, lipid-lowering medications, and strict control of blood pressure and blood sugar.
## Management of Arterial Dissection
If arterial dissection is identified as the underlying cause:
Antiplatelet therapy is commonly used 
Anticoagulation may be considered in selected cases 
Endovascular intervention may be required if symptoms recur despite medical treatment 

## Antiplatelet Therapy
Antiplatelet therapy plays a key role in preventing recurrence:
Dual antiplatelet therapy (Aspirin + Clopidogrel):
Recommended for patients with minor stroke or high-risk transient ischemic attack, ideally started within 24 hours and continued for a short duration (usually 1–3 months). 
Alternative dual therapy (Aspirin + Ticagrelor):
May be considered in selected moderate-risk patients with significant arterial stenosis. 
Long-term therapy typically involves a single antiplatelet agent.
## Role of Anticoagulation
Routine anticoagulant therapy is not generally recommended for watershed strokes unless there is a clear indication, such as a cardioembolic source.
## Supportive and Preventive Care
Long-term management focuses on reducing the risk of recurrence and improving overall vascular health:
Strict blood pressure control 
Management of diabetes and cholesterol levels 
Smoking cessation 
Regular physical activity and healthy diet

# Limitations
This review has several limitations that should be considered while interpreting its findings. A more comprehensive understanding of anatomical variations and the underlying mechanisms of watershed stroke is essential to improve early diagnosis and optimize treatment strategies.

One of the major challenges in current research is the difficulty in precisely identifying the location of watershed infarcts. This is largely due to individual differences in cerebral vascular anatomy, which can lead to variability in the distribution of arterial territories. As a result, defining consistent patterns of border zone regions remains complex.

Additionally, this review does not establish a direct correlation between clinical manifestations and specific anatomical variations in cerebral blood supply. Further research integrating imaging findings with clinical presentation would be valuable in enhancing diagnostic accuracy and guiding personalized management approaches.

# Conclusion
Neuroimaging plays a central role in the diagnosis of stroke, enabling rapid identification of the underlying cause and guiding appropriate treatment decisions. Watershed infarcts are well-recognized on imaging; however, their exact pathophysiological mechanisms are not yet fully understood.

Current evidence suggests that these infarcts result from a combination of reduced cerebral perfusion and embolic events, often originating from unstable atherosclerotic plaques. In many cases, both mechanisms may act together, making it difficult to determine the primary cause of ischemia.

From an anatomical perspective, watershed infarcts are classified into two main types: cortical (external) and internal (deep). Cortical infarcts typically appear as wedge-shaped lesions extending toward the brain surface, whereas internal infarcts may present as linear or scattered patterns within the white matter, sometimes described as “string-like” or “rosary-like” on imaging.

Advanced diagnostic techniques—including diffusion-weighted MRI, perfusion imaging, positron emission tomography, and transcranial Doppler—have significantly improved the understanding of these infarcts and their hemodynamic impact. These tools are valuable in both diagnosis and treatment planning.

Although established guidelines outline medical management and revascularization strategies based on the severity of arterial stenosis, certain aspects—such as optimal blood pressure targets in patients with symptomatic carotid disease—remain unclear. Further research into the mechanisms of watershed infarction is necessary to refine treatment approaches and improve patient outcomes
