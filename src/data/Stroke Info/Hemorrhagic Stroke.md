# Introduction

A cerebrovascular accident (CVA), commonly referred to as a stroke, is the second most common cause of death worldwide and a major contributor to long-term disability. Strokes are broadly classified into two main categories: ischemic and hemorrhagic. Ischemic stroke, the more frequent type, occurs when blood flow to a part of the brain is reduced or completely blocked.

In contrast, hemorrhagic stroke accounts for roughly 10–15% of all stroke cases globally, but it is associated with the highest mortality rate among stroke types. This condition occurs when a blood vessel ruptures, causing bleeding either within the brain tissue or into the space surrounding the brain. Common causes include rupture of weakened blood vessels or disorders related to blood clotting, sometimes linked to anticoagulant therapy.

Hemorrhagic strokes are generally divided into two subtypes: intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) and subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH). Intracerebral hemorrhage involves bleeding directly into the brain tissue, whereas subarachnoid hemorrhage occurs when bleeding develops in the subarachnoid space, the area between the brain and the tissues covering it.

These forms of stroke often lead to significant complications and have a high risk of death. Outcomes tend to worsen as the hemorrhage progresses. The 30-day mortality rate is estimated to be around 30–40%, with nearly half of the deaths occurring within the first 48 hours after onset. Because bleeding can rapidly expand and cause sudden decline in consciousness and neurological function, prompt diagnosis and immediate treatment are critical for improving patient outcomes.

# Etiology

## Hypertension
Hypertension is considered the most important risk factor for hemorrhagic stroke. Long-standing elevation of arterial blood pressure causes structural damage to the small blood vessels of the brain. Persistent pressure forces plasma proteins into the walls of arterioles, producing hyaline arteriosclerosis. With time, these vascular changes lead to degeneration of the vessel wall, disruption of the elastic layer, and fragmentation of smooth muscle cells.

Microscopic changes in the affected vessels may include lipohyalinosis, fibrinoid necrosis, and the formation of small vascular dilatations known as Charcot–Bouchard microaneurysms. These fragile vessels are prone to rupture. Hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage commonly occurs in the small penetrating arteries that branch from major cerebral arteries such as the basilar, anterior cerebral, middle cerebral, and posterior cerebral arteries. These arteries are typically between 50 and 700 micrometers in diameter and may rupture at multiple sites, often showing platelet and fibrin deposition.

Hypertension-related vascular injury usually results in non-lobar intracerebral hemorrhage. In pregnancy-related conditions such as eclampsia, intracerebral hemorrhage may also develop due to impaired regulation of cerebral blood flow. Eclampsia more frequently causes posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) with vasogenic edema, although bleeding within the brain can also occur.

## Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy
Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) is another major cause of lobar intracerebral hemorrhage, particularly in elderly individuals. This condition is characterized by the deposition of amyloid-β protein within the walls of small and medium-sized blood vessels in the cerebral cortex, leptomeninges, and cerebellum. The accumulation weakens the vessel walls, making them more susceptible to bleeding.

CAA has been associated with certain genetic variations, especially those involving the apolipoprotein E gene. A hereditary form may also occur in younger patients and is linked to mutations in the amyloid precursor protein gene. The likelihood of developing CAA increases significantly with age, affecting approximately half of people over 80 years old, and recurrent hemorrhages are common.

Currently, the Boston Criteria 2.0 (introduced in 2022) are widely used for the non-invasive diagnosis of CAA. These criteria rely mainly on MRI findings, including lobar microbleeds and cortical superficial siderosis.

## Other Important Risk Factors
Several additional factors can increase the risk of intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH):
- Smoking, heavy alcohol consumption, and long-term alcoholism significantly increase the risk.
- Chronic liver disease may predispose individuals to ICH due to impaired blood clotting and reduced platelet counts.
- Some studies suggest that very low levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) might be associated with a higher risk, although the evidence is not consistent.
- Dual antiplatelet therapy carries a greater bleeding risk compared with single-drug therapy.
- The use of sympathomimetic substances such as cocaine, heroin, amphetamines, ephedrine, and phenylpropanolamine can elevate the risk of cerebral bleeding.
- Cerebral microbleeds, which may occur in individuals with hypertension, diabetes mellitus, or smoking history, also increase the likelihood of ICH.
- Age and sex influence risk as well. The occurrence of intracerebral hemorrhage rises with advancing age, particularly after 55 years, and the relative risk increases significantly after 70 years. Males are affected more frequently.

Certain brain tumors are also more likely to bleed, including glioblastoma, lymphoma, metastatic tumors, meningioma, pituitary adenoma, and hemangioblastoma.

## Causes of Subarachnoid Hemorrhage
Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) most commonly occurs due to rupture of a cerebral aneurysm. Other causes include arteriovenous malformations, vasculitis, cerebral artery dissection, dural venous sinus thrombosis, and pituitary apoplexy.

Risk factors for SAH include hypertension, oral contraceptive use, substance abuse, and pregnancy. A specific subtype called perimesencephalic non-aneurysmal SAH accounts for approximately 10–20% of cases and generally has a more favorable prognosis.

During pregnancy, intracranial hemorrhage (either intracerebral or subarachnoid) may develop in association with eclampsia, primarily due to impaired regulation of cerebral blood flow.

# Epidemiology

Hemorrhagic stroke represents roughly 10–15% of all stroke cases worldwide, with higher occurrence reported in Asian countries and in low- and middle-income regions. In countries such as the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia, hemorrhagic strokes account for approximately 8–15% of total strokes, whereas the proportion is higher in Japan and Korea, ranging from 18–24%. The estimated annual incidence of hemorrhagic stroke is about 12–15 cases per million people. Rates are particularly elevated in developing regions, especially across parts of Asia. The condition tends to occur more frequently in men, and the risk increases with advancing age.

Globally, the incidence of hemorrhagic stroke appears to be rising, particularly in African and Asian populations. Research conducted in Japan has demonstrated that effective management of hypertension can significantly lower the occurrence of intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH). Mortality rates vary depending on a country's healthcare resources. In high-income nations, the case-fatality rate ranges from 25% to 30%, while in low- and middle-income countries it may reach 30% to 48%. Outcomes are strongly influenced by the availability and quality of critical care services.

The ICH score is commonly used by clinicians to estimate the risk of mortality and to assist in determining the level of medical intervention required. In addition, certain CT scan findings can help predict the likelihood of hematoma enlargement. Radiological indicators such as the spot sign, blend sign, and black hole sign are now considered important factors in early clinical decision-making and treatment planning.

# Pathophysiology and Sites of Bleeding

In intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH), bleeding most frequently occurs in specific regions of the brain. The basal ganglia are the most common site, accounting for approximately 40–50% of cases. Other locations include the thalamus (20–25%), cerebral lobes (15–20%), cerebellum (10–15%), and the pons or brainstem (5–10%).

When bleeding occurs, the resulting hematoma damages surrounding neurons and glial cells. This leads to reduced blood flow in nearby tissues (oligemia), abnormal release of neurotransmitters, mitochondrial dysfunction, and cellular swelling. The clotting process also produces thrombin, which activates microglial cells, initiating inflammatory responses and contributing to the formation of brain edema.

The primary brain injury in hemorrhagic stroke results mainly from the mechanical compression of brain tissue by the hematoma along with a rise in intracranial pressure (ICP).

## Secondary Brain Injury
Beyond the initial damage, a secondary injury process develops. This involves several pathological mechanisms such as:
- Inflammatory reactions
- Breakdown of the blood–brain barrier
- Brain edema
- Excess production of free radicals, including reactive oxygen species
- Excitotoxicity caused by glutamate release
- Release of hemoglobin and iron from the blood clot

Substances such as thrombin, iron, and activated complement proteins play key roles in the damage occurring around the hematoma (perihematomal injury). Because of this, treatments aimed at blocking complement activation or removing excess iron are currently being investigated as possible therapeutic approaches.

Hematoma enlargement typically occurs within the first 3 to 12 hours after the initial bleeding event, with about one-third of cases expanding during the first 3 hours. Perihematomal edema generally begins within 24 hours, reaches its peak around 5–6 days, and may persist for up to two weeks. The area surrounding the hematoma also experiences reduced blood flow (hypoperfusion).

Clinical deterioration in ICH may result from several factors, including:
- Expansion of the hematoma
- Bleeding into the ventricular system (intraventricular hemorrhage)
- Perihematomal edema
- Inflammatory processes

In cases of cerebellar hemorrhage, the hematoma may compress the fourth ventricle, which can obstruct cerebrospinal fluid flow and lead to hydrocephalus during the early stage.

## Non-Aneurysmal Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

Spontaneous non-aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) can be classified into perimesencephalic and non-perimesencephalic types. In perimesencephalic SAH, bleeding is mainly localized to the interpeduncular cistern around the midbrain.

Physical activities that increase intrathoracic pressure, such as Valsalva maneuvers during strenuous exertion, can trigger perimesencephalic SAH by increasing intracranial venous pressure. In contrast, non-perimesencephalic SAH typically shows a more widespread distribution of blood within the subarachnoid space.

# Histopathology

Long-standing chronic hypertension produces characteristic microscopic changes in the small arteries and arterioles of the brain. These alterations include degeneration and loss of smooth muscle cells, thickening of the vessel wall due to hyaline deposition, and fibrinoid necrosis. Over time, the vascular walls become weakened because of hyaline arteriosclerosis, fibrosis, and atrophy of the outer muscular layer.

These structural changes contribute to the formation of small vascular outpouchings known as Charcot–Bouchard microaneurysms, which develop in the penetrating arterioles of the brain. The fragile vessel walls may eventually rupture. In hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH), the main pathological mechanisms involve damage to the arteriolar wall caused by fibrinoid necrosis and rupture of these microaneurysms, leading to bleeding within the brain tissue.

# History and Physical

Stroke commonly presents with symptoms such as headache, speech difficulties (aphasia), weakness on one side of the body (hemiparesis), and facial paralysis. In hemorrhagic stroke, the onset is usually sudden and rapidly progressive. Patients often experience an abrupt severe headache, vomiting, neck stiffness, elevated blood pressure, and quickly developing neurological deficits. The specific symptoms often reflect the location and size of the intracranial bleeding.

Several additional manifestations may occur depending on the affected brain region:
- Severe headache is more frequently associated with large hematomas.
- Vomiting is commonly related to increased intracranial pressure (ICP) and is particularly seen in cerebellar hemorrhage.
- Loss of consciousness or coma may occur when the reticular activating system in the brainstem is involved.
- Seizures, aphasia, and visual field defects (hemianopia) are often observed in lobar hemorrhage. Some patients may experience early warning symptoms such as numbness, tingling, or weakness before the bleeding becomes evident.
- Contralateral sensory and motor deficits are typical in hemorrhages affecting the basal ganglia or thalamus.
- Thalamic hemorrhage may lead to a complete loss of sensation across multiple sensory modalities.
- If a thalamic hematoma extends into the midbrain, it can result in vertical gaze palsy, drooping eyelid (ptosis), and a non-reactive pupil.
- Brainstem hemorrhage may present with cranial nerve abnormalities along with weakness on the opposite side of the body.
- A large pontine hemorrhage frequently leads to coma and paralysis affecting all four limbs (quadriparesis).
- Cerebellar hemorrhage often produces symptoms of raised intracranial pressure, including lethargy, vomiting, and bradycardia. Progressive neurological decline may indicate expansion of the hematoma or increasing cerebral edema.

## Clinical Features of Subarachnoid Hemorrhage
Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) typically presents with a sudden, extremely severe headache, often described as a "thunderclap headache." Other symptoms may include vomiting, fainting (syncope), sensitivity to light (photophobia), neck stiffness, seizures, and reduced consciousness.

Signs of meningeal irritation may also be present. These include:
- **Kernig's sign:** pain when attempting to extend the knee while the hip is flexed at 90 degrees.
- **Brudzinski's sign:** involuntary flexion of the hips when the neck is passively flexed.

# Evaluation of Hemorrhagic Stroke

## Imaging Studies
Computed tomography (CT) is usually the first imaging test performed when intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) is suspected. In the hyperacute stage, the hemorrhage typically shows an attenuation value of about 30–60 Hounsfield units (HU), which may increase to 80–100 HU within a few hours. However, attenuation may appear lower in patients with anemia or coagulation disorders. Surrounding vasogenic edema may gradually increase and persist for up to two weeks.

A non-contrast CT scan is considered the initial imaging method of choice for diagnosing ICH. Although MRI techniques such as gradient-echo and T2 susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) have similar sensitivity to CT in detecting acute hemorrhage and are more sensitive for identifying previous bleeding, they are less commonly used for hyperacute cases because they are slower and less widely available.

During the subacute stage, the hematoma may become isodense with brain tissue, making detection by CT more difficult; in such cases, MRI may be required for better visualization.

## Hematoma Size and Prognosis
The volume of the hematoma can be estimated using the formula:
Volume = (A × B × C) / 2
- **A:** largest diameter of the hematoma
- **B:** diameter perpendicular to A
- **C:** vertical height of the hematoma

A hematoma volume greater than 60 mL is associated with a poor prognosis and higher mortality. Other unfavorable prognostic indicators include:
- Expansion of the hematoma
- Intraventricular hemorrhage
- Infratentorial location
- Contrast leakage on CT angiography (known as the "spot sign")

MRI detects hemorrhage early because deoxyhemoglobin has paramagnetic properties, making it visible on specific imaging sequences. MRI is also helpful in differentiating primary intracerebral hemorrhage from hemorrhagic transformation of an ischemic stroke. In addition, MRI can identify underlying causes such as vascular malformations, cavernomas, tumors, or cerebral venous thrombosis.

## CT Angiography and Other Vascular Imaging
When contrast extravasation is seen on CT angiography (CTA), commonly referred to as the spot sign, it suggests active bleeding and is associated with worse outcomes. Multidetector CTA is also useful in identifying possible causes of secondary hemorrhage, including:
- Arteriovenous malformations (AVMs)
- Ruptured aneurysms
- Dural venous sinus or cerebral vein thrombosis
- Vasculitis
- Moyamoya disease

**Certain imaging patterns may suggest specific underlying conditions:**
- Multiple hemorrhages of different ages in the parieto-occipital region suggest cerebral amyloid angiopathy.
- Bleeding within an arterial territory may indicate hemorrhagic infarction.
- Fluid levels within a hematoma may be associated with anticoagulant-related bleeding.
- Mixed ischemic and hemorrhagic lesions can suggest vasculitis.
- Hemorrhage accompanied by arterial occlusion may indicate Moyamoya disease.

## Digital Subtraction Angiography
In cases of subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH), four-vessel digital subtraction angiography (DSA) is usually required to detect vascular abnormalities. If the first angiogram does not reveal an aneurysm, repeat imaging after 1–2 weeks may be performed, particularly in non-perimesencephalic SAH.

## CT Findings Suggesting Vascular Abnormalities
Certain features on a plain CT scan may indicate an underlying vascular disorder, including:
- Presence of subarachnoid hemorrhage
- Dilated vessels or calcifications around the hemorrhage
- Increased density within a dural venous sinus
- A visible cortical vein along the drainage pathway
- Unusual shape of the hematoma
- Excessive edema relative to the time of hemorrhage
- Atypical location of bleeding
- Presence of additional brain abnormalities, such as a tumor or mass

## Indications for MRI
MRI is particularly useful for detecting secondary causes of hemorrhage, especially when the following features are present:
- Lobar location of hemorrhage
- Age below 55 years
- No history of hypertension

## Evaluation for Cerebral Venous Thrombosis
If cerebral venous thrombosis is suspected, magnetic resonance venography (MRV) or CT venography may be performed. Indications include:
- Unusual hemorrhage location
- Disproportionately large edema
- Abnormal signals in the cerebral venous sinuses

## Laboratory Investigations
Laboratory tests are also important in the evaluation of hemorrhagic stroke. These may include:
- Coagulation studies (clotting time, prothrombin time, activated partial thromboplastin time)
- Platelet count and peripheral blood smear
- Liver and kidney function tests

These investigations help identify coagulation disorders or systemic conditions that may contribute to hemorrhage. To assess for vasculitis, additional tests may be performed, including measurement of:
- Immunoglobulin levels
- Thyroid antibodies
- Rheumatoid factor
- Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCA)
- Anti-endothelial antibodies
- Complement levels
- Anti-Ro and Anti-La antibodies

For patients with intracerebral hemorrhage related to direct oral anticoagulants, specialized tests may also be used when available. These include anti-factor Xa activity levels for drugs such as apixaban or rivaroxaban, and thrombin time for dabigatran.

# Treatment and Management 

The optimal management of hemorrhagic stroke remains a subject of ongoing research and discussion. However, recent clinical trials have improved the understanding of important treatment strategies such as blood pressure control, minimally invasive surgical techniques, and reversal of anticoagulation therapy.

Several major clinical trials have investigated different treatment approaches for hemorrhagic stroke. These include studies such as Antihypertensive Treatment in Acute Cerebral Hemorrhage (ATACH), Intensive Blood Pressure Reduction in Acute Cerebral Hemorrhage Trial (INTERACT), Factor VIIa for Acute Hemorrhagic Stroke Treatment (FAST), and the Surgical Trial in Intracerebral Haemorrhage (STICH). More recent trials—including INTERACT-2, ATACH-2, MISTIE III, and ENRICH (2023–2024)—have contributed significantly to current clinical guidelines and treatment recommendations. Despite these advances, the role of surgery in hemorrhagic stroke continues to be debated.

## Blood Pressure Management

Controlling blood pressure is one of the most important aspects of acute management in hemorrhagic stroke. According to recommendations from the American Stroke Association (ASA), patients who present with systolic blood pressure between 150 and 220 mm Hg may benefit from reducing it to around 140 mm Hg, as this level is considered safe and may lead to better functional recovery.

For patients with systolic blood pressure above 220 mm Hg, a more aggressive reduction using continuous intravenous antihypertensive medication is recommended.

Several classes of medications may be used for this purpose, including:

- Beta-blockers such as labetalol or esmolol
- Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors such as enalapril
- Calcium channel blockers such as nicardipine
- Vasodilators such as hydralazine

During treatment, blood pressure should be monitored frequently, usually every 10 to 15 minutes, to ensure safe and effective control.

Findings from clinical studies have provided mixed results. The ATACH trial found no statistically significant relationship between the extent of systolic blood pressure reduction and outcomes such as hematoma expansion or recovery after three months. In contrast, the INTERACT trial demonstrated that early and intensive lowering of blood pressure could reduce the growth of the hematoma within the first 72 hours after hemorrhage. Overall, research indicates that elevated systolic blood pressure is associated with a higher risk of neurological deterioration and mortality, emphasizing the importance of prompt blood pressure management.

## Management of Increased Intracranial Pressure
The management of elevated intracranial pressure (ICP) is an important component of treatment in patients with hemorrhagic stroke. An initial step involves raising the head of the patient's bed to about 30 degrees, which helps improve venous drainage from the brain and reduce intracranial pressure.

Osmotic agents are commonly administered to decrease brain swelling. Medications such as mannitol and hypertonic saline are frequently used for this purpose. Mannitol (20%) is typically given in a dose ranging from 1.0 to 1.5 g/kg body weight.

In situations where there is a risk of brain herniation, short-term hyperventilation may be used after intubation and mechanical ventilation. This approach lowers the partial pressure of arterial carbon dioxide (PaCO₂) to approximately 30–35 mm Hg, which temporarily reduces intracranial pressure. However, this method is generally considered a temporary measure rather than a long-term treatment.

The American Stroke Association (ASA) recommends monitoring intracranial pressure using either a parenchymal sensor or a ventricular catheter in patients with a Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score below 8, or in those showing signs of transtentorial herniation or hydrocephalus. A ventricular catheter has the additional advantage of allowing drainage of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which can help relieve pressure in cases of hydrocephalus.

During treatment, the goal is to maintain cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) within the range of 50 to 70 mm Hg to ensure adequate blood flow to the brain while minimizing further injury.

## Hemostatic Therapy
Hemostatic therapy is used in the management of hemorrhagic stroke to limit the enlargement of the hematoma. This approach is particularly important in patients who develop bleeding due to anticoagulant-related coagulopathy. The main goal of treatment is to restore normal blood clotting and prevent further bleeding.

Several agents may be used for this purpose, including vitamin K, prothrombin complex concentrates (PCCs), recombinant activated factor VII (rFVIIa), and fresh frozen plasma (FFP). According to recommendations from the American Stroke Association (ASA), patients with low platelet counts (thrombocytopenia) should receive platelet transfusions.

For individuals with prolonged prothrombin time or elevated international normalized ratio (INR), treatment generally involves intravenous vitamin K along with FFP or PCCs to correct the clotting abnormality. Although FFP is effective, it may carry a risk of allergic transfusion reactions. PCCs, which are concentrated plasma-derived products containing clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, are often preferred because they can be rapidly prepared and administered.

Clinical studies have evaluated other hemostatic agents as well. The FAST trial demonstrated that recombinant activated factor VII (rFVIIa) was able to reduce the expansion of hematomas; however, it did not significantly improve survival rates or functional recovery. As a result, rFVIIa is not routinely recommended for all patients, since it does not replace the full range of clotting factors.

For patients receiving the anticoagulant dabigatran, the preferred reversal agent is idarucizumab, which rapidly neutralizes the drug's effects. Another medication, tranexamic acid, has also been studied in the treatment of hyperacute intracerebral hemorrhage. Although it appears to be safe, research has shown that it does not significantly improve long-term functional outcomes. Findings from the Tranexamic Acid for Hyperacute Primary Intracerebral Hemorrhage (TICH) trial indicated that while tranexamic acid did not change functional status at 90 days, it may help reduce hematoma expansion, early mortality, and certain serious complications. Therefore, its use is considered supportive or adjunctive in selected cases.

## Antiepileptic Therapy
Seizures may occur in patients with intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH), particularly during the early phase after the bleeding event. Studies suggest that approximately 3% to 17% of patients develop seizures within the first two weeks, and nearly 30% may show epileptiform activity on electroencephalogram (EEG) monitoring.

Patients who experience clinical seizures or seizures detected on EEG should receive antiepileptic medications. Seizures are more frequently associated with lobar hematomas and expansion of the hemorrhage, which can lead to worsening neurological function. In some cases, subclinical seizures or nonconvulsive status epilepticus may occur, particularly in individuals with a history of epilepsy.

For patients who have reduced levels of consciousness, continuous EEG monitoring is recommended to detect possible hidden seizure activity. However, according to American Stroke Association (ASA) guidelines, routine preventive use of anticonvulsant drugs is not advised unless seizures are documented.

## Surgical Management
Several surgical approaches may be considered in the treatment of hemorrhagic stroke, depending on the size and location of the hemorrhage as well as the patient's clinical condition. Surgical options include craniotomy, decompressive craniectomy, stereotactic aspiration, endoscopic evacuation, catheter-based aspiration, and other minimally invasive procedures.

The potential benefits of surgical intervention include preventing brain herniation, reducing intracranial pressure (ICP), and removing the toxic effects of accumulated blood on surrounding brain tissue.

### Conventional Surgery
The STICH (Surgical Trial in Intracerebral Haemorrhage) study did not demonstrate a clear overall advantage of early surgery compared with initial conservative treatment for supratentorial ICH. However, more recent evidence suggests that early minimally invasive evacuation may be beneficial for certain patients, particularly those with lobar hemorrhages larger than 30 mL, when performed within 24 hours in experienced medical centers. According to ASA guidelines, craniotomy for hematoma removal may be considered as a life-saving procedure in patients who show clinical deterioration.

### Cerebellar Hemorrhage
Emergency surgical treatment is particularly important in cases of cerebellar hemorrhage that produce hydrocephalus or compression of the brainstem. Patients with cerebellar hematomas larger than 3 cm often achieve better outcomes with surgical intervention. Current recommendations suggest urgent evacuation of the hematoma, sometimes combined with external ventricular drainage, when the hemorrhage volume is 15 mL or greater, or when there is brainstem compression or hydrocephalus. Cerebellar hematomas are commonly removed through a suboccipital craniectomy.

### Brainstem Hemorrhage
Surgical evacuation of brainstem hemorrhages is generally not recommended because of the high risk of complications. Nevertheless, some isolated reports describe successful outcomes using procedures such as posterior fossa decompression or stereotactic aspiration.

### Minimally Invasive Procedures
Less invasive techniques, including stereotactic aspiration, are currently being investigated. Some clinical studies have shown potential benefits of stereotactic evacuation, especially in patients with spontaneous putaminal hemorrhage who maintain some level of responsiveness.

Minimally invasive evacuation methods for supratentorial hemorrhages and intraventricular bleeding may help reduce mortality rates. The MISTIE trial examined the use of image-guided catheter techniques combined with rt-PA to remove blood clots. The study found that removal of the clot could reduce perihematomal edema, although improvements in functional recovery were mainly observed when 70% or more of the clot volume was removed. More recently, results from the ENRICH trial indicated that minimally invasive hematoma evacuation may lead to better functional outcomes after 180 days, particularly in patients with lobar hemorrhages.

Another clinical study, the CLEAR IVH trial, evaluated the use of low-dose rt-PA for intraventricular hemorrhage and demonstrated that the treatment could safely accelerate clot breakdown in stable patients.

### Decompressive Craniectomy
In some cases, decompressive craniectomy combined with hematoma evacuation is performed, particularly for patients with large hypertensive hemispheric hemorrhages. Clinical trials have shown that combining decompressive craniectomy with expansive duraplasty may improve outcomes compared with hematoma evacuation alone.

This procedure is generally considered for patients who have:
- Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) scores of 8 or less, and
- Large hematomas exceeding 60 mL in volume.

Careful patient selection is important, as younger individuals tend to benefit more from the procedure. In addition, hemorrhages affecting the dominant cerebral hemisphere may carry a higher risk of severe disability. Overall, decompressive craniectomy may reduce mortality and improve neurological outcomes in selected patients. According to ASA recommendations, this procedure can be considered as a life-saving intervention.

# Cerebroprotection

In hemorrhagic stroke, brain damage is not limited to the initial bleeding. A secondary injury process develops due to mechanisms such as inflammation, oxidative stress, and the toxic effects of substances released from damaged red blood cells and thrombin. Various therapeutic approaches are being explored to minimize these harmful effects.

Some medications have been investigated for their anti-inflammatory properties, including pioglitazone, misoprostol, and celecoxib, which may help reduce inflammation-related damage in brain tissue. Other compounds aim to decrease oxidative stress, a process that contributes to further neuronal injury. Agents such as edaravone, flavonoids, and nicotinamide mononucleotide have been studied for their antioxidant effects. Additionally, deferoxamine, an iron-chelating drug, is being examined in experimental studies because excess iron released from blood breakdown products can worsen brain injury. 

The compound citicoline, which is a component of cell membranes, has shown potential neuroprotective effects and good safety profiles in several studies. Another medication, rosuvastatin, which inhibits the enzyme HMG-CoA reductase, has been associated with improved clinical outcomes in some clinical trials. In cases of aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage, the drug nimodipine is commonly used to reduce the risk of delayed cerebral ischemia. Its beneficial effect is mainly related to vascular mechanisms, rather than direct neuroprotection. Despite ongoing research, no neuroprotective drug has yet demonstrated a clear improvement in functional outcomes in large phase III clinical trials.

# General Care

Effective medical management, nursing support, and rehabilitation play a crucial role in the overall treatment of patients with hemorrhagic stroke. Comprehensive care is essential to prevent complications and improve recovery.

Several medical complications may occur during the course of the illness, including difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), aspiration, cardiac arrhythmias, stress-related cardiomyopathy, heart failure, acute kidney injury, gastrointestinal bleeding, and urinary tract infections. Careful monitoring and early intervention are necessary to manage these conditions. In patients who are unable to swallow safely, percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) may be required to provide adequate nutrition and prevent aspiration. However, PEG placement is typically delayed for about 7 to 14 days to allow time for possible neurological improvement before making long-term feeding decisions. 

Evaluation for cardiac complications is also important. Screening for myocardial ischemia using electrocardiography (ECG) and cardiac enzyme tests is recommended in patients with hemorrhagic stroke. To reduce the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), the use of intermittent pneumatic compression devices is advised. In contrast, elastic compression stockings have shown limited effectiveness and are not strongly recommended. 

Early involvement of a multidisciplinary rehabilitation team—including physiotherapists, occupational therapists, and speech therapists—can help minimize long-term disability and improve functional outcomes. In addition, blood glucose levels should be monitored carefully. Both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia should be avoided, as abnormal glucose levels may negatively affect neurological recovery.

# Differential Diagnosis

Several medical conditions may present with symptoms similar to those of hemorrhagic stroke and should therefore be considered during evaluation. These include:
- Severe hypertensive crisis
- Pituitary apoplexy
- Cerebral venous thrombosis
- Dural venous sinus thrombosis
- Cervical artery dissection
- Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome
- Hemorrhagic brain tumors
- Arteriovenous malformations
- Meningitis
- Acute subdural hematoma
- Hemorrhagic transformation of an ischemic infarct

To distinguish hemorrhagic stroke from these conditions, neuroimaging techniques such as computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are essential, as they help identify the presence and cause of intracranial bleeding.

# Prognosis

The outcome of intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) depends on several clinical and radiological factors. Indicators associated with a poor prognosis include coma at presentation, large hematoma volume (greater than 30 mL), intraventricular extension of bleeding, hemorrhage in the posterior fossa, advanced age (over 80 years), elevated blood glucose levels, and chronic kidney disease. Early neurological deterioration and death are common complications in patients with ICH. The presence of coma at the time of admission generally suggests a particularly severe outcome.

The American Stroke Association (ASA) recommends that patients with intracerebral hemorrhage receive treatment and monitoring in a specialized stroke unit to improve outcomes. Despite appropriate care, recovery can be limited; studies indicate that only about 20% of patients regain independence within six months. Some survivors may remain in a persistent vegetative state following extensive hemispheric damage, while brainstem involvement may result in locked-in syndrome.

## ICH Score
The ICH score, developed by Hemphill and colleagues, is commonly used to estimate the risk of mortality in patients with intracerebral hemorrhage. The score is calculated based on several clinical and radiological variables:

**Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS):**
- 3–4 → 2 points
- 5–12 → 1 point
- 13–15 → 0 points

**Age:**
- ≥80 years → 1 point
- <80 years → 0 points

**Location of hemorrhage:**
- Infratentorial → 1 point
- Supratentorial → 0 points

**Hematoma volume:**
- >30 mL → 1 point
- ≤30 mL → 0 points

**Presence of intraventricular hemorrhage:**
- Present → 1 point
- Absent → 0 points

The total score correlates with the estimated 30-day mortality rate:
- Score 0 → 0% mortality
- Score 1 → 13% mortality
- Score 2 → 26% mortality
- Score 3 → 72% mortality
- Score 4 → 97% mortality
- Score 5–6 → 100% mortality

Another prognostic model, the Functional Outcome in Patients with Primary Intracerebral Hemorrhage (FUNC) score, is used to estimate the likelihood of functional independence after hemorrhagic stroke and is often used alongside the ICH score to better assess patient outcomes.

# Complications

Patients with intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) may develop several complications during the course of the illness. Common neurological complications include cerebral edema, increased intracranial pressure (ICP), hydrocephalus, and seizures. Systemic complications such as venous thromboembolism, hyperglycemia, elevated blood pressure, fever, and infections can also occur.

Extension of bleeding into the ventricular system and the development of hydrocephalus are relatively frequent, affecting approximately 30% to 50% of individuals with ICH. In addition, patients with intracerebral hemorrhage—particularly women—have a higher risk of thromboembolic events. To reduce the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), the American Stroke Association (ASA) recommends the use of intermittent pneumatic compression devices as a preventive measure.

Pulmonary complications are also common and occur in about one-third of patients. These may include pneumonia, aspiration, pulmonary edema, respiratory failure, and acute respiratory distress.

Cardiac complications are less frequent but may still occur in roughly 4% of cases. These can include myocardial infarction, atrial fibrillation, ventricular arrhythmias, stress-related cardiomyopathy, and acute heart failure.

In subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH), additional complications may arise, such as cerebral vasospasm, delayed cerebral ischemia, recurrent bleeding, seizures, hyponatremia, and hydrocephalus. Another condition associated with SAH is neurogenic pulmonary edema, which results from the accumulation of fluid in the interstitial and alveolar spaces of the lungs.

# Prevention and Patient Education

Intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) has a risk of recurrence, particularly in individuals with certain underlying risk factors. Advanced age and uncontrolled hypertension are among the most important contributors to repeated bleeding episodes. Therefore, maintaining effective blood pressure control is essential to reduce the likelihood of recurrence.

In conditions such as cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA), the risk of recurrent hemorrhage remains relatively high even with adequate blood pressure management, and currently there is no definitive disease-modifying treatment available for this condition.

Patients should be encouraged to adopt healthy lifestyle habits to minimize further risk. These include avoiding smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and the use of illicit drugs. In addition, ongoing multidisciplinary rehabilitation is important to support recovery and improve functional ability after stroke.

Several factors may increase the probability of recurrent intracerebral hemorrhage, including:
- Lobar location of the initial hemorrhage
- Older age
- Presence and higher number of cerebral microbleeds detected on gradient-echo MRI
- Continued use of anticoagulant medications
- Genetic factors, such as the presence of apolipoprotein E (APOE) ε2 or ε4 alleles

## Clinical Pearls and Additional Considerations

Lifestyle modification plays an important role in both the primary and secondary prevention of intracerebral hemorrhage. Recommended measures include regular physical activity, smoking cessation, avoidance of recreational drug use, limiting alcohol intake, and maintaining a balanced diet. A healthy diet should emphasize fish rich in long-chain omega-3 fatty acids, vegetables, fruits, and whole-grain foods, while reducing the consumption of red meat and foods high in added sugars. In addition, saturated fats should be replaced with polyunsaturated or monounsaturated fats. Maintaining strict blood pressure control below 130/80 mm Hg is considered one of the most effective strategies for preventing recurrent hemorrhagic stroke.

Chronic cerebral small-vessel disease is another important condition related to intracerebral hemorrhage. It is characterized by pathological changes such as lipohyalinosis, fibrinoid necrosis, and reduction in small blood vessels. These abnormalities can often be identified on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Typical MRI findings include small subcortical infarcts, white matter hyperintensities in deep or periventricular regions, hemosiderin deposits from previous hemorrhages, and cerebral microbleeds (CMBs).

The location of microbleeds can provide clues about the underlying cause. Deep microbleeds are commonly associated with hypertensive vascular changes, whereas lobar microbleeds are more characteristic of cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA). Additional MRI features of CAA may include cortical superficial siderosis, often related to previous convexity subarachnoid hemorrhage, as well as white matter hyperintensities predominantly in the posterior regions of the brain.

Proper blood pressure management remains one of the most important strategies for reducing the risk of intracerebral hemorrhage in individuals with small-vessel disease.

# Enhancing Healthcare Team Outcomes

Hemorrhagic stroke is a serious cerebrovascular condition caused by bleeding within the brain tissue (intracerebral hemorrhage) or into the subarachnoid space. This type of stroke is associated with high rates of illness and death. Patients may experience rapid neurological decline due to factors such as expansion of the hematoma, increased intracranial pressure, seizures, or hydrocephalus. Prompt recognition of symptoms, appropriate diagnostic imaging, and the use of evidence-based treatments—such as careful blood pressure control, management of intracranial pressure, and rapid reversal of coagulation abnormalities—are essential for improving patient outcomes. In addition, distinguishing between intracerebral hemorrhage and subarachnoid hemorrhage and identifying underlying causes such as hypertension, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, or vascular abnormalities are important for guiding appropriate treatment strategies.

Effective care for hemorrhagic stroke requires a collaborative, interprofessional approach involving multiple healthcare professionals. Physicians and advanced practice providers are responsible for interpreting imaging findings, initiating urgent medical therapies, and determining whether surgical or minimally invasive procedures are necessary. Nurses play a vital role in ongoing neurological assessment, monitoring blood pressure, and identifying early signs of clinical deterioration. Pharmacists contribute by ensuring the safe and timely administration of medications, including drugs used for blood pressure control and reversal of anticoagulation. In addition, rehabilitation specialists, therapists, and care coordinators help develop recovery plans and support long-term functional improvement. Clear communication and teamwork among healthcare professionals improve patient safety, reduce complications, and promote coordinated, patient-centered care.
