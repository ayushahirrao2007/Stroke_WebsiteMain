### Overview

Cerebellar infarcts are a subtype of acute ischemic stroke resulting from occlusion of one of the three principal cerebellar arteries within the vertebrobasilar circulation: the superior cerebellar artery (SCA), anterior inferior cerebellar artery (AICA), or posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA). Although posterior circulation strokes account for approximately 20% of all ischemic strokes, cerebellar infarcts represent only about 3% of cases in the United States.

This is primarily due to their often subtle and nonspecific early clinical presentation, which can delay diagnosis. Additionally, the confined space of the posterior fossa predisposes patients to life-threatening complications such as brainstem compression and obstructive hydrocephalus due to reactive edema.

This educational activity is designed to enhance healthcare professionals’ competence in the evaluation and management of cerebellar infarcts. It aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of the condition’s pathophysiology, clinical features, diagnostic strategies, and evidence-based management. Improved knowledge and skills will enable clinicians to work effectively within interprofessional teams, ensuring timely intervention and optimizing patient outcomes.

**Recognize early clinical features** of cerebellar infarction, including vertigo, impaired balance (ataxia), severe headache, nausea/vomiting, altered mental status, and drowsiness, to facilitate prompt diagnosis.

**Evaluate the need for advanced neurosurgical interventions**, such as:

**Promote interprofessional collaboration** among healthcare providers to improve early detection, decision-making, and comprehensive patient care, ultimately enhancing functional outcomes and quality of life.

# Introduction: Cerebellar Infarcts
Cerebellar infarcts are a subtype of acute ischemic stroke caused by occlusion of one of the three principal cerebellar arteries within the vertebrobasilar (posterior circulation) system: the superior cerebellar artery (SCA), anterior inferior cerebellar artery (AICA), and posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA). Posterior circulation strokes account for approximately 20% of all acute ischemic strokes, while cerebellar infarcts represent only about 3% of ischemic strokes in the United States.

Despite their relatively low incidence, cerebellar infarcts are associated with considerable morbidity and mortality. This is largely due to their often subtle and nonspecific early clinical presentation, which can delay diagnosis and treatment. Furthermore, the restricted anatomical space of the posterior fossa increases the risk of serious complications, including brainstem compression and obstructive hydrocephalus caused by reactive edema.

Typically, occlusion of one of the major cerebellar arteries results in large, symptomatic cortical and subcortical infarcts. However, with the widespread use of brain magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), smaller cerebellar infarcts are increasingly being identified. These lesions are often cortical, may not conform to specific vascular territories, and can remain clinically silent. In many cases, such small infarcts are discovered incidentally on MRI as ischemic cortical cavities, without prior neurological symptoms.

Cerebellar infarcts require special clinical attention due to their unique diagnostic complexities and management challenges, emphasizing the importance of early recognition and appropriate intervention.

# Etiology of Cerebellar Infarcts
Cerebellar infarcts result from obstruction of one of the three principal cerebellar arteries: the posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA), anterior inferior cerebellar artery (AICA), or superior cerebellar artery (SCA). Similar to other ischemic strokes, multiple vascular mechanisms can contribute to the development of cerebellar infarction.

The major underlying etiologies include:
- **Large artery atherosclerosis**, particularly involving the vertebral or basilar arteries
- **Artery-to-artery embolism** originating from proximal atherosclerotic plaques
- **Cardioembolism**, often due to cardiac conditions such as atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter
- **Arterial dissection**, especially involving the vertebral arteries
- **Trauma** affecting cervical vasculature
- **Small vessel disease**
- **Other less common causes**, including vasculitis and fibromuscular dysplasia

Among these, **vertebrobasilar atherosclerotic disease** and **cardioembolism** are the most frequent, collectively accounting for approximately 75% of acute cerebellar infarcts.

Cardioembolic events typically arise from impaired cardiac function or abnormal cardiac rhythms, which disrupt normal blood flow and promote clot formation. Conditions such as atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter are particularly important contributors.

Smaller cerebellar infarcts (less than 2 cm in size), which are often asymptomatic, were historically considered border-zone (watershed) infarcts occurring between major cerebellar arterial territories. However, current evidence also supports small vessel disease as a significant cause of these lesions. With the increasing use of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), such infarcts are frequently detected incidentally as ischemic cortical cavities, even in individuals without overt neurological symptoms.

# Epidemiology of Cerebellar Infarcts
Cerebellar infarcts account for approximately 2%–3% of all strokes, making them a relatively uncommon subtype of ischemic stroke. Despite their low incidence, they are associated with disproportionately high morbidity and mortality compared to other stroke types.

Evidence from a large study involving nearly 2,000 consecutive stroke patients demonstrated that cerebellar strokes carry a mortality rate of approximately 23%, which is significantly higher than that observed in more common cerebral (supratentorial) strokes, reported at 12.5%. Brainstem strokes exhibit an intermediate mortality rate of around 17%.

The increased mortality associated with cerebellar infarcts is primarily attributed to:
- **Delayed diagnosis, due to subtle or nonspecific early symptoms**
- **Rapid neurological deterioration, particularly from edema in the posterior fossa**
- **Life-threatening complications, such as brainstem compression and obstructive hydrocephalus**
These findings highlight the importance of early recognition, close monitoring, and timely intervention in patients with suspected cerebellar infarction.

# Pathophysiology of Cerebellar Infarcts
The neurological deficits observed in cerebellar infarcts are primarily determined by the specific vascular territory involved, as each cerebellar artery supplies distinct 
anatomical and functional regions.

**Posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA) infarction**, the most common type, typically presents with sudden onset of vertigo, dizziness, and inability to stand or walk (ataxia). 

It may also result in lateral medullary (Wallenberg) syndrome, characterized by:
- Hoarseness and dysarthria
- Ipsilateral facial loss of pain and temperature sensation
- Contralateral loss of pain and temperature in the body
- Horner syndrome

Anterior inferior cerebellar artery (AICA) infarction affects both cerebellar and brainstem structures, leading to:
- Dysmetria and ataxia
- **Unilateral hearing loss or tinnitus** (due to involvement of the internal auditory artery)
- Ipsilateral facial paralysis or sensory loss
- Contralateral loss of pain and temperature sensation in the body

**Superior cerebellar artery (SCA) infarction**, which involves more rostral regions, is commonly associated with:
- Prominent ataxia
- Dysarthria
- Nystagmus
- Less frequent symptoms such as vertigo, headache, and vomiting

It is important to note that clinical presentations may overlap and can sometimes be atypical, complicating early diagnosis.

### Cerebral Edema and Complications

Following an acute cerebellar infarction, cerebral edema develops in two phases:

- Cytotoxic edema – occurs early due to cellular energy failure, leading to intracellular swelling
- Vasogenic edema – develops later due to disruption of the blood–brain barrier, allowing fluid to leak into the extracellular space 

Edema in cerebellar infarcts is particularly dangerous due to the restricted space of the posterior fossa, which is bounded by:

- The tentorium cerebelli (superiorly)
- The occipital bone (posteriorly)
- The foramen magnum (inferiorly)
- The brainstem and fourth ventricle (anteriorly)

As swelling progresses, it can lead to life-threatening complications:

- Upward transtentorial herniation: Compression of the brainstem due to upward displacement of the cerebellar vermis
- Obstruction of the fourth ventricle: Leading to obstructive hydrocephalus and increased intracranial pressure
- Direct brainstem compression: Causing rapid neurological deterioration
- Tonsillar herniation: Downward displacement of cerebellar tonsils through the foramen magnum, which can be fatal

These pathophysiological mechanisms highlight why cerebellar infarcts, although relatively rare, require urgent recognition, close monitoring, and timely intervention to prevent catastrophic outcomes.

# History and Physical Examination in Cerebellar Infarcts

The most characteristic presentation of an acute cerebellar infarct includes the sudden onset of vertigo, nausea, vomiting, and gait ataxia. In elderly patients, acute vertigo accompanied by inability to walk should raise strong suspicion for a cerebellar infarct until proven otherwise. Similarly, when vertigo is associated with headache and inability to walk, a cerebellar hemorrhage must be ruled out. A high index of clinical suspicion is essential for early diagnosis and prevention of complications.

Approximately 75% of patients with cerebellar infarcts experience dizziness, often described as vertigo or a sensation of falling toward the affected side. Many patients report difficulty walking, typically due to ataxia or weakness. Additionally, more than half present with nausea and vomiting. Notably, the severity of symptoms may appear disproportionate to physical findings, making diagnosis more challenging.

### Neurological Examination Findings

Common neurological signs include:

- **Ataxia** (truncal or limb)
- **Nystagmus**, particularly:
    - Direction-changing
    - Gaze-evoked
    - Vertical or torsional
- **Diplopia** (double vision)
- **Dysarthria** (slurred speech)
- **Cranial nerve deficits**

A key distinguishing feature is that deficits are typically ipsilateral (on the same side as the lesion), unlike many cerebral strokes.

In more severe cases, particularly with large infarcts or increased intracranial pressure, patients may develop:

- Lethargy
- Reduced consciousness or coma
- Cardiovascular instability, indicating brainstem involvement and poor prognosis

### Clinical Challenges and Risk Factors

Cerebellar infarcts are frequently misdiagnosed or overlooked due to their nonspecific presentation, which can mimic other conditions. The clinical picture varies depending on the location and size of the infarct, making a thorough history and detailed neurological examination essential.

The presence of vascular risk factors significantly increases suspicion, including:

- Advanced age
- Smoking
- Obesity
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hyperlipidemia
- Hypertension

### Differential Considerations

Symptoms of cerebellar infarction often overlap with other serious conditions. Therefore, clinicians must carefully evaluate and rule out alternative diagnoses such as:

- Trauma
- Aortic dissection
- Acute coronary syndrome
- Pulmonary embolism
- Hypovolemia
- Sepsis

A comprehensive systemic evaluation is critical to avoid missing other life-threatening conditions.

### Evaluation of Cerebellar Infarcts

Accurate and timely diagnosis of cerebellar infarction relies on a combination of neuroimaging, clinical examination, and supportive investigations.

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) is the gold standard for diagnosing cerebellar infarction. It is highly sensitive in detecting early ischemic changes, including areas of restricted diffusion and poor perfusion, allowing for precise localization of infarcted tissue.

Magnetic resonance angiography (MRA) further enhances evaluation by identifying vascular occlusions, particularly in large vessels such as the basilar artery, and helps guide endovascular interventions.

### Role of Computed Tomography (CT)

Noncontrast computed tomography (CT) is typically the initial imaging modality, mainly due to its widespread availability and rapid acquisition. It is particularly useful for:

- Excluding hemorrhage
- Identifying alternative diagnoses
- Occasionally detecting early signs of infarction

However, CT has limited sensitivity for cerebellar infarcts due to beam-hardening artifacts from the surrounding temporal and occipital bones, which reduce image resolution in the posterior fossa.

When MRI is not available, CT angiography (CTA) and CT perfusion imaging serve as valuable alternatives for:

- Detecting vascular occlusions
- Assessing cerebral blood flow and perfusion deficits

### Clinical Bedside Evaluation

In addition to imaging, careful bedside assessment is crucial. The HINTS examination—which includes:

- Head Impulse test
- Nystagmus assessment
- Test of Skew (alternate eye cover test)

—can help differentiate central causes of vertigo (such as cerebellar infarction) from peripheral causes. The presence of skew deviation strongly suggests a central pathology.

# Treatment and Management of Cerebellar Infarcts

Management of cerebellar infarcts follows general principles of acute ischemic stroke care, with additional emphasis on early detection of complications, particularly cerebral edema and brainstem compression.

### 1. Acute Reperfusion Therapy

Patients presenting within the appropriate time window may benefit from reperfusion therapies:

- **Intravenous thrombolysis (rtPA)**
    - Indicated if administered within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
    - Aims to restore cerebral blood flow and limit infarct size
- **Mechanical thrombectomy**
    - Considered in large vessel occlusions, especially basilar artery occlusion
    - May be performed beyond the conventional 6-hour window in posterior circulation strokes
    - Decision is often guided by imaging showing:
    - Perfusion–diffusion mismatch
    - Adequate collateral circulation

Posterior circulation structures may tolerate ischemia slightly better due to higher white matter content and collateral flow, allowing for an extended therapeutic window in selected cases.

### 2. Antithrombotic Therapy

If reperfusion therapy is not feasible:

- Aspirin is initiated early
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin + clopidogrel) may be considered in selected patients
- Anticoagulation is indicated in cases of cardioembolic stroke (e.g., atrial fibrillation)

### 3. Monitoring and Supportive Care

Cerebellar infarcts require close monitoring, preferably in a neurological intensive care unit (ICU), especially during the first 72 hours, when edema peaks.
Key aspects include:

- **Continuous neurological assessment**
- **Monitoring for:**
    - Decreased level of consciousness
    - New cranial nerve deficits
    - Gaze abnormalities
    - Signs of raised intracranial pressure

- **Early symptoms of deterioration may include:**
    - Severe headache
    - Vomiting
    - Drowsiness
    - Altered mental status

### 4. Management of Cerebral Edema

Cerebral edema typically worsens 3–4 days post-infarction and can lead to life-threatening complications.


**Medical management:**
- Mannitol
- Hypertonic saline
- Controlled hyperventilation (temporary measure)

### 5. Neurosurgical Interventions

In patients with large infarcts and increased intracranial pressure, urgent neurosurgical management may be required:

- External ventricular drain (EVD) / ventriculostomy
- Decompressive suboccipital craniectomy
- Removal of infarcted tissue or associated hematoma (in selected cases)

These interventions are critical in preventing:

- Brainstem compression
- Obstructive hydrocephalus
- Herniation

### 6. Evaluation of Underlying Cause

During hospitalization, efforts should be made to identify and treat the underlying etiology:

- **Cardiac monitoring** → atrial fibrillation or arrhythmias
- **Echocardiography** → embolic sources (e.g., patent foramen ovale)
- **Laboratory tests** → diabetes, hyperlipidemia, coagulation disorders

### 7. Criteria for Intravenous Thrombolysis (rtPA)

**Inclusion Criteria**

- Age ≥18 years 
- Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke with measurable neurological deficit 
- Treatment within 4.5 hours of symptom onset (or last known well time) 

**Exclusion Criteria**

**Clinical:**

- Suspected subarachnoid hemorrhage
- Active internal bleeding
- Infective endocarditis
- Aortic dissection
- Severe uncontrolled hypertension (≥185/110 mmHg)

**Imaging:**

- Evidence of intracranial hemorrhage
- Extensive infarction on CT

**History:**

- Recent stroke or head trauma (within 3 months)
- Prior intracranial hemorrhage
- Recent major surgery
- Gastrointestinal bleeding (within 3 weeks)

**Hematologic:**

- INR >1.7
- Platelet count <100,000/mm³
- Recent use of anticoagulants (LMWH, direct thrombin or factor Xa inhibitors)

# Differential Diagnosis of Cerebellar Infarcts

Cerebellar infarctions are frequently misdiagnosed due to their nonspecific and overlapping clinical presentation. Common conditions that may mimic cerebellar stroke include:

- **Vestibular disorders** such as vestibular neuronitis
- **Migraine**, particularly vestibular migraine
- **Syncope**, including vasovagal syncope or cardiac arrhythmias
- **Metabolic disturbances**, such as hypoglycemia
- **Toxic causes**, including alcohol or drug intoxication
- **Hypertensive emergencies**
- **Renal failure** and other systemic illnesses

Careful evaluation of the onset, progression, and associated factors is critical for accurate diagnosis and risk stratification.

### Temporal Pattern of Symptoms

The time course of symptom onset provides important diagnostic clues:

- **Seconds to minutes**
    - Suggests:
    - Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)
    - Vasovagal syncope
    - Cardiac arrhythmias
- **Minutes to hours**
    - Suggests:
    - Hypoglycemia
    - Migraine
    - Psychological or functional disorders
- **Hours to days**
    - Suggests:
    - Labyrinthitis
    - Medication side effects
    - Cerebellar infarction (should be strongly suspected)

### Broad Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of cerebellar infarction includes:

- **Intracranial hemorrhage**, particularly cerebellar hemorrhage
- **Brainstem infarction**
- **Lacunar stroke**
- **Middle cerebral artery (MCA) stroke**
- **Multiple sclerosis**
- **Hypertensive encephalopathy**
- **Migraine headache**
- **Toxic-metabolic disturbances** (e.g., hypoglycemia, drug or alcohol intoxication)
- **Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES)**
- **Seizure with postictal weakness (Todd paralysis)**

### Clinical Importance
Because cerebellar infarcts can present with subtle and nonspecific symptoms, they are often overlooked or mistaken for benign conditions. Therefore, thorough history-taking, detailed neurological examination, and appropriate neuroimaging are essential to differentiate cerebellar infarcts from other conditions.

Early and accurate differentiation is crucial, as delayed diagnosis can lead to severe complications, including brainstem compression and increased mortality.

# Prognosis of Cerebellar Infarcts

The prognosis of cerebellar infarction is generally comparable to that of other types of stroke, with outcomes largely dependent on the size and location of the infarct. Larger infarctions involving extensive cerebellar territory are associated with higher morbidity and mortality, primarily due to complications such as brainstem compression, obstructive hydrocephalus, and increased intracranial pressure.

Advancements in early recognition, neuroimaging, and timely intervention have contributed to a gradual reduction in overall morbidity and improved survival rates in recent years.

Patients with small, localized cerebellar infarcts typically have a favorable prognosis, often achieving good functional recovery. In contrast, individuals with large or symptomatic infarcts are more likely to experience persistent neurological deficits and poorer outcomes.

Early diagnosis and appropriate management play a critical role in improving both short-term survival and long-term neurological outcomes in patients with cerebellar infarction.

### Complications of Cerebellar Infarcts
Delayed diagnosis and management of cerebellar infarction can result in progressive cerebral edema, which may lead to brainstem compression, herniation, and death. These complications are particularly dangerous due to the limited space within the posterior cranial fossa.

With early recognition and appropriate treatment, most patients experience favorable outcomes; however, residual functional deficits remain a common complication, particularly in cases of larger infarcts.

### Acute Hospital-Related Complications
Patients with cerebellar infarction are also at risk of complications commonly seen in other stroke patients during hospitalization, including:

- **Venous thromboembolism (VTE)**
- **Pressure ulcers (bedsores)**
- **Urinary tract infections (UTIs)**
- **Pneumonia**, often due to immobility or aspiration

These complications are associated with prolonged hospitalization and increased morbidity.

# Postoperative and Rehabilitation Care

Rehabilitation following cerebellar infarction is a comprehensive, multidisciplinary, and individualized process aimed at restoring functional independence and improving quality of life. Patients who undergo surgical interventions, such as decompressive suboccipital craniectomy, require intensive monitoring in an acute care setting during the immediate postoperative period.

### Early Rehabilitation

Early initiation of rehabilitation—often beginning during hospitalization—is critical for optimizing recovery outcomes. Depending on the patient’s condition, rehabilitation may continue in:

- Specialized rehabilitation centers
- Outpatient programs
- Home-based care settings

An interprofessional team plays a central role in rehabilitation, typically including:
- Physiotherapists
- Occupational therapists
- Speech and language therapists
- Neurologists and rehabilitation physicians

### Rehabilitation Components

1. **Motor and Coordination Training**
- Focuses on improving balance, gait, and coordination, which are commonly affected due to cerebellar dysfunction 
- Includes exercises to enhance posture control and reduce fall risk 

2. **Cognitive Rehabilitation**
- Addresses impairments in: 
- Memory
- Attention
- Problem-solving skills
- Helps patients regain independence in daily activities

3. **Speech and Swallowing Therapy**
- Management of dysarthria and dysphagia, if present 
- Improves communication and reduces aspiration risk 

4. **Assistive Devices**
- Use of: 
- Canes
- Walkers
- Orthotic devices
- Enhances mobility, safety, and independence

5. **Environmental Modifications**
- Home assessments to ensure a safe living environment 
- Includes: 
- Fall prevention strategies
- Installation of support aids (e.g., handrails)

### Psychosocial Support

Psychological and emotional support is a vital component of recovery. Stroke can significantly impact both patients and caregivers, leading to:

- Depression
- Anxiety
- Emotional distress

Counseling and support services help in addressing these challenges and improving overall well-being.

### Follow-Up and Long-Term Care

Ongoing monitoring is essential to track recovery progress and adjust the rehabilitation plan as needed. Regular follow-ups with:

- Neurologists
- Rehabilitation specialists

ensure continuous evaluation and optimization of care.

### Consultations in Cerebellar Infarcts

Management of cerebellar infarction requires a multidisciplinary and interprofessional approach to ensure timely diagnosis, appropriate treatment, and optimal patient outcomes.

Initial evaluation and triage are typically performed in the emergency department by a physician or advanced practitioner, who assesses the patient’s neurological status and determines the urgency of care.

Depending on the severity of the condition, further management may involve:

- Neurology specialists, who play a central role in diagnosis, medical management, and stroke evaluation

- Intensivists or internal medicine physicians, who oversee critical care management, especially in patients with severe neurological compromise 

- Neurosurgeons, who are consulted in cases requiring surgical intervention, such as: 
    - Decompressive suboccipital craniectomy
    - External ventricular drainage (EVD) for hydrocephalus

### Importance of Interprofessional Collaboration

Effective coordination among healthcare professionals ensures:

- Rapid diagnosis and treatment initiation
- Continuous monitoring for complications
- Timely surgical intervention when indicated
- Comprehensive management of underlying risk factors

# Deterrence and Patient Education

Patient education is a crucial component in the secondary prevention of cerebellar infarction. Educating patients about modifiable risk factors and preventive strategies significantly reduces the risk of recurrent strokes and improves long-term outcomes.

### Key Components of Patient Education

1. **Lifestyle Modifications**

Patients should be encouraged to adopt a heart-healthy lifestyle, including:

- A balanced diet low in saturated fats and salt
- Regular physical activity
- Weight management

2. **Risk Factor Control**

Strict management of underlying comorbidities is essential:

- Hypertension
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hyperlipidemia

3. **Smoking Cessation**

Patients should be strongly advised to quit smoking, as it is a major modifiable risk factor for stroke. 

4. **Medication Adherence**

Emphasis on strict compliance with prescribed medications, including: 

- Antiplatelet agents
- Anticoagulants (if indicated)
- Antihypertensive and lipid-lowering agents

### Role of Healthcare Providers

Healthcare professionals play a vital role in:

- Educating patients and caregivers
- Reinforcing adherence to treatment plans
- Encouraging regular follow-up and monitoring
- Promoting awareness of stroke warning signs for early intervention

# Enhancing Healthcare Team Outcomes

Patients with cerebellar infarction often present with nonspecific or atypical symptoms, and clinical features may overlap with other neurological and systemic conditions, particularly in cases of hemorrhagic transformation. Due to this variability, a high index of clinical suspicion and strong diagnostic expertise are essential for timely recognition and management.

### Importance of an Interprofessional Team

Effective management of cerebellar infarcts relies on a coordinated interprofessional approach involving:

- Physicians and neurologists
- Emergency medicine specialists
- Neurosurgeons
- Nurses
- Pharmacists
- Rehabilitation specialists

### Role of Collaborative Care

An interprofessional team enables:

- Comprehensive patient assessment
- Rapid decision-making in emergency situations
- Timely initiation of treatment, including thrombolysis or surgical intervention
- Identification of appropriate specialist involvement based on disease severity
- Continuous monitoring for complications and treatment response

## Impact on Patient Outcomes

The interprofessional model significantly enhances patient care by ensuring:

- Early diagnosis and intervention
- Reduction in complications and mortality
- Improved functional recovery
- Better coordination of acute and long-term care
