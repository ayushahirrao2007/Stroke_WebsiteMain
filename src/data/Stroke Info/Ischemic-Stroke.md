# What is Ischemic Stroke?

Acute ischemic stroke is a life-threatening medical emergency that occurs when blood flow to a specific region of the brain is suddenly interrupted, leading to focal neurological deficits. Previously known as a cerebrovascular accident (CVA), stroke remains one of the leading causes of death and long-term disability worldwide. Because of its significant impact on individuals and healthcare systems, understanding its causes, risk factors, and current treatment approaches is essential for improving outcomes.

Ischemic strokes account for approximately 87% of all stroke cases. They occur when a blood vessel supplying the brain becomes obstructed, depriving brain tissue of oxygen and nutrients. This blockage may develop directly within a cerebral artery (thrombosis) or result from a clot or debris that forms elsewhere in the body and travels to the brain (embolism). In some cases, the original source of the embolus cannot be identified.

Rapid recognition and intervention are critical because brain cells begin to die within minutes of losing blood supply. Evaluation includes a detailed neurological examination and urgent brain imaging. A non-contrast computed tomography (CT) scan is typically performed first to exclude bleeding, while diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) can more sensitively detect early ischemic changes when available.

Initial management focuses on stabilizing vital functions, including airway, breathing, and circulation. Eligible patients may receive intravenous thrombolytic therapy within 4.5 hours of symptom onset to dissolve the clot. For selected individuals with large vessel occlusions, mechanical thrombectomy may be performed within up to 24 hours, depending on imaging findings and clinical criteria.

Ischemic strokes are commonly linked to atherosclerosis, a condition in which fatty deposits (plaque) accumulate along arterial walls. Over time, plaque narrows and stiffens arteries, reducing blood flow. When plaque forms in the carotid arteries of the neck—major vessels supplying the brain—it can lead to carotid artery disease, a significant contributor to stroke risk. If plaque ruptures, platelets adhere to the injured area and form a clot that may partially or completely obstruct blood flow.

Certain medical conditions also increase stroke risk. Atrial fibrillation, an irregular heart rhythm, can allow clots to form in the heart and travel to the brain. Sickle cell disease is another important risk factor, particularly in children. Imaging studies indicate that many children with sickle cell disease experience "silent" strokes—brain injuries detected on MRI without obvious neurological symptoms. Regular blood transfusions are currently the primary preventive treatment for these silent infarcts.

There have been reports of ischemic strokes occurring in individuals with COVID-19 infection; however, ongoing research is needed to determine whether the virus directly increases stroke risk or whether these events are related to other factors.

A transient ischemic attack (TIA), often referred to as a "mini-stroke," occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is temporarily blocked but restored before permanent damage develops. Symptoms resemble those of a stroke but usually resolve within minutes to an hour. Although no lasting injury occurs, a TIA is a warning sign of a potential future stroke and requires prompt medical evaluation and follow-up with a primary care provider and a neurologist.

To better understand the underlying cause of an ischemic stroke, clinicians often use the TOAST (Trial of Org 10172 in Acute Stroke Treatment) classification system, which groups strokes based on their presumed mechanism. This system helps guide treatment decisions and secondary prevention strategies.

The major categories include:
- **Cardioembolism** – Stroke resulting from a clot that forms in the heart and travels to the brain.
- **Small vessel occlusion** – Also known as lacunar stroke, caused by blockage of small penetrating arteries within the brain.
- **Large artery atherosclerosis** – Stroke due to narrowing or blockage of major extracranial or intracranial arteries from atherosclerotic plaque.
- **Stroke of undetermined cause** – Cases in which no clear source is identified despite evaluation, or when multiple possible causes are present.
- **Stroke of other determined cause** – Less common causes, such as hypercoagulable states, arterial dissection, or other specific conditions identified through diagnostic testing.

Within this framework, a subtype may be described as probable when the patient's clinical presentation, imaging findings, and diagnostic studies consistently support a specific cause and alternative explanations have been excluded. A subtype is considered possible when the available clinical and imaging data suggest a likely cause, but confirmatory testing has not been completed or remains inconclusive.

Because not all patients undergo extensive diagnostic evaluation, this classification system allows healthcare providers to assign the most accurate and practical etiologic category based on the information available.

# Etiology of Ischemic Stroke

Ischemic stroke develops when cerebral blood flow is interrupted by either a locally formed clot or material that travels from another location in the body. In thrombotic events, the blockage forms directly within the affected artery, most often as a consequence of atherosclerosis, arterial dissection, fibromuscular dysplasia, or inflammatory vascular disorders. In contrast, embolic events occur when circulating debris migrates through the bloodstream and lodges in a cerebral vessel, obstructing perfusion.

Emboli may arise from a nearby large artery—such as a ruptured plaque within the internal carotid artery—and travel downstream to smaller intracranial branches (artery-to-artery embolism). More frequently, the heart serves as the source. In certain situations, clots originating from the venous system may enter the arterial circulation through a right-to-left cardiac shunt, such as a patent foramen ovale, ultimately reaching the brain. Identifying the underlying mechanism is clinically important because stroke cause influences recurrence risk, treatment strategy, and long-term prognosis.

## Cardioembolic Stroke

This subtype includes cases in which cerebral artery occlusion is presumed to result from a clot formed within the heart. Cardiac conditions are categorized as high or moderate risk based on their likelihood of generating emboli. For a stroke to be attributed to a cardiac origin, at least one potential cardiac source must be identified.

Clinical presentation and imaging patterns may resemble those seen in large artery disease. Clues supporting a cardiac origin include prior transient ischemic attacks affecting different vascular territories, infarctions involving multiple brain regions, or evidence of emboli in other organs. Competing causes—particularly significant atherosclerotic narrowing of major arteries—must be excluded. When a patient has a moderate-risk cardiac condition and no alternative explanation is identified, the event may be classified as a possible cardioembolic stroke.

## Large Artery Atherosclerosis

This category involves strokes associated with substantial narrowing (greater than 50%) or complete blockage of a major extracranial or intracranial artery due to atherosclerotic plaque. Symptoms often reflect cortical dysfunction, such as language impairment, neglect, or motor deficits, or may involve the brainstem or cerebellum.

Supportive historical findings may include limb claudication, repeated TIAs within the same vascular distribution, audible carotid bruits, or weakened peripheral pulses. Imaging typically reveals infarcts affecting the cerebral cortex, cerebellum, brainstem, or larger subcortical regions exceeding approximately 1.5 cm in diameter.

Vascular studies—such as duplex ultrasound or angiographic imaging—should demonstrate significant stenosis in the artery supplying the affected territory. A cardiac source of embolism must also be ruled out. If vascular imaging shows only minimal irregularities or normal findings, this subtype cannot be assigned.

## Small Vessel Occlusion (Lacunar Stroke)

This group consists of infarcts involving small penetrating arteries, commonly referred to as lacunar strokes. Patients typically present with characteristic lacunar syndromes and lack signs of cortical involvement, such as aphasia or visual field deficits. A background of chronic hypertension or diabetes mellitus often supports this diagnosis.

Brain imaging may appear normal initially or show a small lesion—usually less than 1.5 cm—within the deep subcortical structures or brainstem. Evaluation should confirm the absence of a cardiac embolic source and exclude significant narrowing of major extracranial arteries on the same side.

## Stroke of Undetermined Cause

In many instances, establishing a definitive explanation for a stroke is not possible. Some individuals undergo comprehensive diagnostic testing without identification of a likely source, while others receive limited evaluation. This category also includes patients with more than one plausible cause, making it difficult to determine the primary mechanism.

For example, a patient with both atrial fibrillation and moderate ipsilateral carotid narrowing, or someone with features of a lacunar syndrome alongside notable carotid stenosis, would be placed in this group because no single cause can be confidently assigned.

## Stroke of Other Determined Cause

This final category encompasses less frequent aetiologies, including nonatherosclerotic vascular disorders, inherited or acquired clotting abnormalities, and certain blood diseases. Clinical symptoms and imaging findings must be consistent with acute cerebral infarction, regardless of lesion size or location.

Laboratory studies, specialized imaging, or angiographic evaluations should confirm one of these uncommon mechanisms. As with other categories, competing causes—such as significant large artery disease or cardiac embolism—must be excluded before this classification is made.

# Epidemiology of Ischemic Stroke

Stroke remains one of the leading causes of death and long-term disability worldwide.

Ischemic stroke accounts for approximately 85–87% of all stroke cases, making it the predominant subtype. Globally, stroke ranks among the top causes of mortality and is a major contributor to years of life lost and years lived with disability.

## Incidence and Prevalence

In the United States, nearly 800,000 individuals experience a stroke each year. The majority are first-time events, while roughly one-quarter occur in individuals with a prior history of stroke. On average, a stroke occurs approximately every 40 seconds.

Despite improvements in prevention and acute management, stroke continues to affect a substantial portion of the population, particularly older adults.

Incidence rates increase markedly with advancing age. Although stroke can occur at any age, risk doubles with each decade after age 55. As life expectancy increases globally, the absolute number of strokes is expected to rise, even in regions where age-adjusted incidence rates are declining.

## Mortality

Stroke remains a leading cause of cardiovascular death. Although mortality rates declined for several decades due to better control of hypertension, smoking cessation efforts, and improved acute care, recent data suggest a plateau or slight increase in stroke-related deaths in certain populations. Case fatality rates are highest within the first 30 days following the event, particularly among older individuals and those with large infarcts or significant comorbid conditions.

## Disability Burden

Stroke is a major cause of long-term neurological disability. Many survivors experience persistent motor weakness, speech impairment, cognitive deficits, or reduced functional independence. Among individuals aged 65 years and older, more than half of stroke survivors have lasting limitations in mobility. The condition therefore contributes significantly to healthcare utilization, rehabilitation needs, and caregiver burden.

## Economic Impact

The financial cost of stroke is substantial. In the United States alone, the annual economic burden—including hospital care, rehabilitation services, medications, and lost productivity—exceeds tens of billions of dollars. Indirect costs from disability and reduced workforce participation contribute significantly to the overall economic impact.

## Racial and Ethnic Disparities

Marked disparities exist in stroke incidence, recurrence, and mortality. Non-Hispanic Black adults have nearly twice the risk of experiencing a first stroke compared with White adults. Stroke mortality rates are also higher in Black and Pacific Islander populations. These differences are multifactorial and reflect variations in the prevalence of hypertension, diabetes, access to healthcare, socioeconomic factors, and structural inequities.

Hispanic populations also demonstrate higher stroke incidence at younger ages compared with non-Hispanic White populations. Geographic disparities are notable within the United States, particularly in the southeastern region often referred to as the "Stroke Belt," where incidence and mortality rates are elevated.

## Global Trends

Worldwide, low- and middle-income countries now account for the majority of stroke cases and deaths. While high-income countries have seen reductions in age-adjusted stroke rates due to improved prevention strategies, developing regions continue to experience rising stroke incidence, largely driven by increasing rates of hypertension, diabetes, obesity, and tobacco use.

## Risk Factor Distribution

Hypertension remains the most significant modifiable risk factor for ischemic stroke across all populations. Atrial fibrillation is increasingly recognized as a major contributor to stroke in aging populations. Diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, smoking, sedentary lifestyle, and obesity further increase risk. Effective management of these conditions has been central to the observed decline in stroke incidence in certain regions.

# Pathophysiology of Ischemic Stroke

Ischemic stroke occurs when blood flow to a part of the brain is reduced or completely blocked, depriving brain tissue of oxygen and nutrients. Within minutes, affected neurons begin to malfunction and may eventually die. The major mechanisms include cerebral thrombosis, cerebral embolism, and lacunar infarction.

## 1. Cerebral Thrombosis (Large-Artery Ischemic Stroke)

Cerebral thrombosis develops when a blood clot forms directly within one of the arteries supplying the brain. The blockage usually occurs at the site of pre-existing vessel disease.

**Most Common Cause:** The leading underlying cause is large-vessel atherosclerosis, a condition in which fatty deposits (atheromatous plaques) accumulate within arterial walls. Over time, these plaques narrow the vessel lumen and may rupture, triggering clot formation that obstructs blood flow.

Large arteries commonly involved include:
- The internal carotid arteries
- The vertebral and basilar arteries
- Major intracranial arteries such as the middle cerebral artery

**Additional Risk Factors** — besides atherosclerosis, other contributing conditions include:
- Vasculitis (inflammation of blood vessels)
- Arterial dissection (a tear in the vessel wall)
- Hypercoagulable states (inherited or acquired clotting disorders)
- Smoking
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hypertension
- Hyperlipidemia
- Sedentary lifestyle and obesity

**Clinical Features:** Symptoms often evolve gradually over minutes to hours and depend on the brain region affected. They may include:
- Weakness or paralysis on one side of the body
- Speech disturbances (aphasia or dysarthria)
- Visual field defects
- Sensory loss
- Cognitive impairment

## 2. Cerebral Embolism (Embolic Stroke)

An embolic stroke occurs when a clot or other material forms elsewhere in the body and travels through the bloodstream to lodge in a cerebral artery, suddenly blocking circulation.

**Most Common Source — The Heart:** The heart is the most frequent origin of emboli. Cardioembolic strokes are commonly associated with:
- Atrial fibrillation (irregular heart rhythm causing blood stasis in the atria)
- Recent myocardial infarction
- Valvular heart disease (e.g., mitral stenosis)
- Prosthetic heart valves
- Infective endocarditis

In atrial fibrillation, blood can pool in the left atrium, forming a thrombus that may dislodge and travel into the cerebral circulation.

**Less Common Sources** — other potential embolic materials include:
- Venous clots (via paradoxical embolism in patients with a patent foramen ovale)
- Septic emboli from infection
- Fat emboli (e.g., after long bone fractures)
- Air emboli (e.g., trauma or invasive procedures)
- Tumor emboli (rare)

**Clinical Characteristics:** Embolic strokes typically produce sudden and severe symptoms without warning. Neurological deficits often reach maximum intensity at onset.

## 3. Lacunar Infarcts (Small Vessel Stroke)

Lacunar infarcts are small, deep brain infarctions caused by occlusion of small penetrating arteries. These vessels supply subcortical structures and have limited or no collateral circulation, making the tissue especially vulnerable.

### Commonly Affected Arteries
- Lenticulostriate branches of the middle cerebral artery
- Thalamic perforating arteries from the posterior cerebral artery
- Paramedian branches of the basilar artery

### Typical Locations
- Basal ganglia
- Internal capsule
- Thalamus
- Pons

**Underlying Pathology:** The primary cause is small vessel arteriolosclerosis, often related to:
- Chronic hypertension (most significant risk factor)
- Aging
- Diabetes mellitus
- Smoking
- Dyslipidemia

Long-standing hypertension leads to thickening and narrowing of small arterial walls (lipohyalinosis), ultimately reducing blood flow.

**Clinical Syndromes** — lacunar strokes often produce characteristic syndromes, such as:
- Pure motor stroke
- Pure sensory stroke
- Ataxic hemiparesis
- Dysarthria–clumsy hand syndrome

Although small in size, multiple lacunar infarcts over time can contribute to vascular dementia and gait disturbances.

## Pathophysiology of Brain Injury

When cerebral blood flow falls below critical levels:
- Energy production fails.
- Cellular ion pumps malfunction.
- Excess glutamate is released, causing excitotoxicity.
- Calcium influx triggers cell death pathways.
- Inflammation and oxidative stress worsen tissue damage.

The central core of severely reduced blood flow becomes irreversibly infarcted, while the surrounding region (the ischemic penumbra) may remain viable if reperfusion occurs quickly.

## Diagnosis

Evaluation typically includes:
- Non-contrast CT scan (to rule out hemorrhage)
- MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging (more sensitive for early ischemia)
- CT or MR angiography to assess vessel patency
- Cardiac evaluation (ECG, echocardiography)
- Blood tests for metabolic and coagulation abnormalities

## Prevention and Risk Reduction

Prevention strategies depend on the underlying mechanism and may include:
- Blood pressure control
- Statin therapy for cholesterol reduction
- Antiplatelet medications (e.g., aspirin)
- Anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation
- Smoking cessation
- Diabetes management
- Regular physical activity
- Healthy diet (e.g., Mediterranean diet)

## Concept of the Ischemic Penumbra

During an acute ischemic stroke, interruption of blood flow through a cerebral artery leads to the formation of an infarct core — the central region of brain tissue that suffers severe and sustained loss of perfusion. This tissue is typically supplied predominantly by a single artery and, once blood flow falls below a critical threshold for a sufficient duration, irreversible cell death occurs.

Surrounding this core is the ischemic penumbra, a zone of functionally impaired but still viable brain tissue. In this region, blood flow is reduced but partially maintained through collateral circulation (alternative vascular pathways). Although neurons in the penumbra cannot function normally due to insufficient oxygen and glucose, they remain structurally intact for a limited period and are potentially salvageable if reperfusion is achieved promptly.

# Cerebral Blood Flow & Ischemic Penumbra

Under normal physiological conditions, cerebral blood flow averages approximately 50 mL per 100 g of brain tissue per minute.

Different thresholds of perfusion produce different outcomes:
- **>50 mL/100 g/min** – Normal neuronal function
- **20–50 mL/100 g/min** – Electrical dysfunction occurs, but cells remain viable
- **<15 mL/100 g/min** – Energy failure and irreversible neuronal injury

Brain cells begin to undergo irreversible damage when perfusion drops below roughly 30% of normal levels (approximately 15 mL/100 g/min). When blood flow remains reduced but above this critical threshold, the tissue is ischemic but not yet infarcted—this area constitutes the penumbra.

## Dynamic Evolution of the Penumbra

The ischemic penumbra is not static. Its fate depends on:
- Duration of ischemia
- Adequacy of collateral circulation
- Systemic blood pressure
- Blood glucose levels
- Body temperature

As cytotoxic edema develops within the infarct core, local swelling and inflammatory processes may further compromise surrounding perfusion. Without timely restoration of blood flow, the penumbra progressively converts into infarcted tissue, causing the core to expand.

## Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Within the penumbra:
- ATP production declines
- Ion pump failure leads to cellular depolarization
- Excess glutamate release causes excitotoxicity
- Calcium influx activates destructive enzymatic pathways
- Oxidative stress and inflammation exacerbate injury

However, because some perfusion persists, these processes may be reversible in the early stages.

## Clinical Significance: "Time is Brain"

The concept of the ischemic penumbra forms the scientific foundation for modern acute stroke therapy. The widely recognized principle "time is brain" reflects the reality that millions of neurons are lost each minute that large-vessel ischemia persists.

Rapid revascularization treatments aim to salvage the penumbra before it transitions to irreversible infarction. These treatments include:
- Intravenous thrombolysis (within established time windows)
- Mechanical thrombectomy for selected large-vessel occlusions
- Optimization of systemic factors such as oxygenation and blood pressure

Advanced imaging techniques—such as diffusion-weighted MRI and perfusion imaging—can help distinguish the infarct core from the penumbra, allowing clinicians to tailor treatment decisions even beyond traditional time windows in selected patients.

# Ischemic Stroke Syndromes: MCA, ACA & PCA

Ischemic strokes produce recognizable clinical patterns depending on which cerebral artery is affected. Because specific brain regions control distinct motor, sensory, visual, and language functions, careful neurological examination often allows clinicians to localize the vascular territory involved.

## Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Infarction

The middle cerebral artery (MCA) is the artery most frequently involved in ischemic stroke. It supplies a large portion of the lateral cerebral hemisphere as well as important deep brain structures.

The MCA is divided into four segments:
- **M1 (horizontal segment)** – Gives rise to the lenticulostriate arteries that supply the basal ganglia and internal capsule.
- **M2 (Sylvian segment)** – Travels within the Sylvian fissure and supplies the insula, lateral frontal lobe, superior temporal lobe, and parietal regions.
- **M3 (opercular segment)** – Courses over the operculum.
- **M4 (cortical branches)** – Supplies the lateral cortical surface.

### Classic Clinical Features of MCA Stroke
- Contralateral hemiparesis (weakness on the opposite side of the body)
- Contralateral facial weakness
- Contralateral sensory loss, especially affecting the face and upper extremity
- Gaze preference toward the side of the lesion

### Additional Findings
- **Dysarthria** – Slurred or poorly articulated speech from weakness of speech muscles. Unlike aphasia, language comprehension remains intact.
- **Hemispatial Neglect** – Most common in nondominant (right) hemisphere strokes. Patients fail to attend to stimuli on the opposite side.
- **Visual Field Defects** – Parietal lobe involvement causes inferior quadrantanopia; temporal lobe involvement causes superior quadrantanopia ("pie-in-the-sky"). Extensive lesions may cause homonymous hemianopia.
- **Aphasia** – Occurs when the dominant (usually left) hemisphere is affected:
  * **Broca aphasia**: impaired speech production, preserved comprehension
  * **Wernicke aphasia**: fluent but nonsensical speech, impaired comprehension
  * **Global aphasia**: severe impairment of both production and comprehension

## Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) Infarction

The ACA supplies the medial surfaces of the frontal and parietal lobes, including medial motor/sensory cortex and supplementary motor area. Medial cortex corresponds to the contralateral lower extremity on the homunculus.

Isolated ACA infarctions are relatively uncommon due to robust collateral circulation via the anterior communicating artery.

### Clinical Presentation
- Contralateral leg weakness (hemiparesis) — hallmark feature
- Contralateral leg sensory loss
- Relative sparing of the face and upper extremity
- Abulia (lack of initiative or motivation)
- Personality or behavioral changes
- Impaired judgment
- Urinary incontinence (medial frontal micturition centers)
- Gait apraxia

Bilateral ACA infarcts, though rare, may result in akinetic mutism or severe lower limb weakness.

## Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA) Infarction

The PCA supplies the occipital lobe, inferomedial temporal lobe, thalamus, and midbrain. It is divided into superficial (cortical) and deep branches.

### Superficial (Cortical) PCA Infarcts
— present with:
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (frequently with macular sparing)
- Visual agnosia (difficulty recognizing objects)
- Prosopagnosia (difficulty recognizing faces)
- Alexia without agraphia (dominant hemisphere, splenium involvement)

### Deep PCA Infarcts (Thalamic Involvement)
— present with:
- Contralateral hemisensory loss
- Altered consciousness or hypersomnolence
- Memory impairment
- Ocular movement abnormalities
- Ataxia
- Hemiparesis (if internal capsule involved)

### Bilateral PCA Infarction
— rare, can cause:
- Cortical blindness (intact pupillary reflexes)
- Profound memory disturbances
- Visual disorientation

Posterior circulation strokes often present with subtle or fluctuating symptoms such as dizziness, imbalance, or altered mental status. Clinicians should maintain a high index of suspicion and pursue prompt neuroimaging.

# Vertebrobasilar, Cerebellar & Lacunar Infarction

## Vertebrobasilar Infarction

Vertebrobasilar infarction refers to ischemic stroke in the posterior circulation, which supplies the brainstem, cerebellum, occipital lobes, thalamus, and parts of the temporal lobes. This territory is formed by the paired vertebral arteries and the basilar artery.

**Anatomy:**
- Vertebral arteries arise from the subclavian arteries, ascend through C6–C1, and enter the skull via the foramen magnum. They give off the PICA (inferior cerebellum and lateral medulla) and anterior spinal artery.
- The two vertebral arteries unite at the pontomedullary junction to form the basilar artery.
- Basilar artery gives rise to: AICA (lateral pons, anterior inferior cerebellum), paramedian penetrating branches (brainstem), and SCA (superior cerebellum and upper brainstem).
- At the upper border of the pons, the basilar artery divides into the posterior cerebral arteries (PCAs), which connect to the anterior circulation via posterior communicating arteries, completing the Circle of Willis.

**Common Causes:**
- Atherosclerotic thrombosis
- Cardioembolism (e.g., atrial fibrillation)
- Vertebral artery dissection
- Small vessel disease
- Hypercoagulable states

**Common Symptoms:**
- Ataxia (impaired coordination)
- Vertigo (spinning sensation)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Headache
- Dysarthria (slurred speech)
- Diplopia (double vision)
- Visual field deficits
- Oculomotor abnormalities
- Oropharyngeal dysfunction
- Weakness or sensory changes
- Altered consciousness (in severe cases)

## Cerebellar Infarction

Cerebellar infarction occurs when blood flow to the cerebellum is interrupted, often due to occlusion of PICA, AICA, or SCA.

**Clinical Features:**
- Ataxia (truncal or limb)
- Severe vertigo
- Nausea and vomiting
- Occipital headache
- Dysarthria
- Nystagmus

**Complications:** Cerebellar edema can compress the brainstem, obstruct CSF flow, cause hydrocephalus, and lead to rapid neurological deterioration. Early recognition is critical.

## Lacunar Infarction

Lacunar infarcts are small ischemic strokes (usually <1 cm) caused by occlusion of small penetrating arteries, commonly associated with chronic hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and small vessel disease (lipohyalinosis).

**Common Locations:** Internal capsule, thalamus, basal ganglia, pons.

**Classic Lacunar Syndromes:**
1. Pure motor stroke – weakness without sensory loss
2. Pure sensory stroke – sensory loss without weakness
3. Sensorimotor stroke – combined weakness and sensory loss
4. Ataxic hemiparesis – weakness with incoordination
5. Dysarthria–clumsy hand syndrome

Lacunar strokes typically do not cause cortical signs such as aphasia, neglect, or visual field deficits.

## Diagnosis & Management

Diagnosis includes: MRI with DWI (most sensitive), CT scan (emergency), CT/MR angiography, cardiac evaluation.

**Acute Treatment:**
- Intravenous thrombolysis (if within treatment window)
- Mechanical thrombectomy (selected large vessel occlusions)
- Antiplatelet therapy
- Anticoagulation (if cardioembolic source identified)
- Airway protection, blood pressure management, cerebral edema management
- Neurosurgical decompression (in severe cerebellar swelling)

**Key Points:**
- The vertebrobasilar system supplies the posterior brain circulation.
- Cerebellar infarctions can deteriorate rapidly due to edema.
- Lacunar infarcts are small vessel strokes with characteristic clinical syndromes.
- Early imaging and treatment significantly improve outcomes.

# History, Physical Exam & Stroke Evaluation

## History and Physical Examination

Ischemic stroke typically presents with sudden onset of neurological symptoms. Establishing the exact time symptoms began is critical. If the precise onset is unclear, clinicians use the "last known well" time — the moment the patient was last seen without new neurological deficits — as the reference point for determining thrombolytic eligibility.

Important vascular risk factors include hypertension, diabetes mellitus, cigarette smoking, and a prior history of stroke or TIA. A thorough cardiac history is essential, particularly regarding atrial fibrillation, recent myocardial infarction, or cardiomyopathy. Additional factors include recent neck injury, chiropractic manipulation, and hypercoagulable conditions.

Stroke severity is measured using the NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) — a standardized 11-component tool scored 0–42 assessing:
- Level of consciousness (responsiveness to questions and commands)
- Eye movements and visual fields
- Facial muscle function
- Motor strength in the arms and legs
- Limb coordination
- Sensation, language, speech clarity, and attention

## Acute Ischemic Stroke: Initial Emergency Evaluation

Implementation of a structured stroke protocol is strongly recommended. Target door-to-needle time for IV thrombolysis is 60 minutes or less.

**Immediate Priorities:**
- Stabilizing airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs)
- Rapid identification and correction of reversible conditions
- Determining eligibility for IV thrombolysis and/or mechanical thrombectomy
- Initiating workup to determine stroke mechanism

Supplemental oxygen is recommended if oxygen saturation falls below 94%. Hypotension and significant arrhythmias should be corrected promptly. A bedside capillary glucose must be performed immediately — hypoglycemia can mimic stroke and is rapidly reversible.

## Neuroimaging

**Non-contrast CT:** Should be completed within 20 minutes of arrival to exclude intracranial hemorrhage. CT angiography (CTA) or MR angiography (MRA) may identify large-vessel occlusion for thrombectomy planning, but must not delay thrombolytic administration.

**Diffusion-Weighted MRI (DWI):** Detects early ischemic injury within minutes of onset. A DWI-positive / FLAIR-negative mismatch suggests stroke within ~4.5 hours — useful for wake-up strokes with unknown onset time.

**CT Perfusion:** May identify salvageable penumbra in selected patients. Thrombectomy is generally considered within 6 hours of onset and up to 24 hours in selected patients based on perfusion imaging.

## Laboratory and Cardiac Evaluation

- **ECG:** Detects atrial fibrillation, myocardial ischemia, arrhythmias
- **Cardiac troponin:** Elevated levels may indicate concurrent myocardial injury
- **Complete blood count (CBC):** Anemia, infection, thrombocytopenia
- **Serum electrolytes:** May mimic neurological deficits if abnormal
- **BUN and creatinine:** Important before contrast administration
- **Coagulation studies (PT, INR, PTT):** Essential before thrombolysis
- **Pregnancy testing** in women of childbearing age before thrombolytics

## Blood Pressure Management

- **Before thrombolysis:** BP must be <185/110 mmHg
- **Without thrombolysis:** Permissive hypertension allowed (up to 220/120 mmHg) to maintain cerebral perfusion

## Telemedicine & Additional Considerations

Telestroke services are recommended in facilities without on-site stroke specialists for rapid neurological and radiologic consultation. Patients should be kept NPO until formal swallow evaluation. Early antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin) is recommended within 24–48 hours in patients not treated with thrombolytics. DVT prophylaxis should be considered in immobilized patients.

# Acute Stroke Treatment & Management

## Therapeutic Goal

The primary objective in acute ischemic stroke management is to salvage the ischemic penumbra — brain tissue that is hypoperfused but still viable. Early reperfusion limits infarct size, improves functional outcomes, and reduces long-term disability.

Recanalization strategies include:
- Intravenous fibrinolytic therapy
- Mechanical thrombectomy
- Advanced endovascular techniques

## Intravenous Alteplase (Within 4.5 Hours)

IV alteplase (recombinant tPA) is recommended for eligible patients presenting within 3 hours of symptom onset, extending to 4.5 hours in selected patients.

**Dosing:** 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) — 10% as IV bolus over 1 minute, remaining 90% infused over 60 minutes.

**Basic Eligibility:** Clinical ischemic stroke diagnosis, measurable neurological deficit, age ≥18 years, within time window.

**Major Contraindications:** Active internal bleeding, known intracranial hemorrhage, recent intracranial surgery or severe head trauma, severe uncontrolled hypertension, known bleeding diathesis.

For 3–4.5 hour window, additional relative exclusions: age ≥80, NIHSS >25, oral anticoagulant use, history of both diabetes and prior ischemic stroke.

## MRI-Guided Thrombolysis for Unknown Onset Stroke

The WAKE-UP trial showed patients with DWI-positive / FLAIR-negative MRI pattern can benefit from alteplase. DWI becomes positive within minutes; FLAIR changes appear after ~4.5 hours. This mismatch suggests stroke within the therapeutic window.

## Intravenous Tenecteplase (TNK)

Tenecteplase is an alternative fibrinolytic with a longer half-life, single IV bolus administration, and greater fibrin specificity. Administered as a weight-based single bolus (typically 0.25 mg/kg). Current guidelines indicate TNK may be preferred in patients also candidates for mechanical thrombectomy.

## Mechanical Thrombectomy

**Within 6 Hours:** Recommended for eligible patients with large vessel occlusion (LVO) in the anterior circulation. Multiple 2015 landmark trials established thrombectomy within 6 hours significantly improves outcomes vs. medical therapy alone.

**Extended Window (6–24 Hours) with Perfusion Imaging:**
- **DAWN trial:** Benefit up to 24 hours with clinical–imaging mismatch
- **DEFUSE 3 trial:** Supported intervention up to 16 hours

Current recommendations: Thrombectomy within 6–16 hours for eligible patients; up to 24 hours in DAWN criteria patients.

**Thrombectomy in Large Core Infarcts (2023):**
- ANGEL-ASPECT and SELECT 2 trials showed improved outcomes with thrombectomy in ASPECTS score 3–5, NIHSS >6, large LVO strokes within 24 hours.

## ASPECTS Score

Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score — 10-point CT-based scoring for MCA territory strokes. Start at 10, subtract 1 point per region showing early ischemic change. Regions: Caudate, Putamen, Internal capsule, Insula, Cortical regions M1–M6. Lower score = larger infarct.

## Basilar Artery Occlusion

Carries ~80% poor outcome if untreated.
- **ATTENTION trial:** Benefit within 12 hours
- **BAOCHE trial:** Thrombectomy supported between 6–24 hours
Current evidence supports thrombectomy up to 24 hours in eligible patients.

## Key Clinical Takeaways

- **"Time is brain"** — rapid reperfusion is critical.
- **IV Thrombolysis** remains standard care within 4.5 hours for eligible patients.
- **Mechanical Thrombectomy** is the standard for LVOs up to 6 hours and up to 24 hours based on advanced imaging.
- **Stroke Units**: Admitting patients to dedicated stroke units significantly reduces mortality and dependency rates.
