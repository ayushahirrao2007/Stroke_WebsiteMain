# Hypertension Role in Stroke

![Hypertension and Stroke](/assets/images/cvs/1-HTN-and-Stroke.png)

Hypertension is the most important modifiable risk factor for stroke, causing over 50% of strokes worldwide. It causes strokes by accelerating atherosclerosis (narrowing vessels) or weakening arteries, leading to ischemic (clot) or hemorrhagic (bleeding) events. Managing blood pressure (BP) requires urgent care in acute stages to protect the brain, followed by long-term management to prevent recurrence. 

## Hypertension and Stroke Connection 

- **Ischemic Stroke**: High BP causes atherosclerosis, making vessels narrow and stiffer, leading to clotting.
- **Hemorrhagic Stroke**: Intense pressure causes damaged blood vessels to rupture, causing brain bleeds.
- **Risk Factors**: High BP is associated with 54% of strokes globally; it damages vessels over time. 

## What Is High Blood Pressure? 
High blood pressure affects about half of people in the world and often has no noticeable symptoms. Left untreated, it damages blood vessels and can lead to a number of serious diseases including stroke. More than half of all strokes are associated with hypertension or high blood pressure. A simple blood pressure check can determine whether you have high blood pressure and a health professional can advise on whether your condition can be managed with lifestyle changes and/or the right medication. 

## How Is High Blood Pressure Diagnosed? 
The only way to check for high blood pressure is to have it checked. When you have a blood pressure check, your health professional will record two numbers known as systolic and diastolic pressure. Systolic pressure, the higher number, is a measure of the force with which blood is being pumped around your body. Diastolic pressure is the level of resistance to the blood flow in your body.

## How Is High Blood Pressure Linked to Stroke? 
Consistently high or elevated blood pressure makes your circulatory system – your heart, arteries and blood vessels – work harder at their job of moving blood around your body. This extra work causes damage that can make your blood vessels, including those in your brain, harder and narrower over time. This process, known as atherosclerosis, increases the risk of blockages in the blood vessels in your brain. If the blood supply to an area of the brain is cut off or restricted it can lead to permanent brain damage, or death. Atherosclerosis can also increase the pressure the blood flows through blood vessels in the brain causing them to burst and bleed. This is called a haemorrhagic stroke which can also cause permanent damage to the brain or death.

## What Causes High Blood Pressure?
High (elevated) blood pressure is linked to:
- Age – if you are aged over 65 you are at higher risk of elevated blood pressure 
- Genetics – if you have a family history of hypertension you are more at risk 
- Ethnicity – if you are from a Polynesian, AfroCaribbean or South Asian background you are at higher risk of high blood pressure 
- Pregnancy – if you are pregnant hormonal changes put you at increased risk of elevated blood pressure and stroke 
- Health conditions – people with diabetes are likely to have elevated blood pressure; kidney disease can be a result of elevated blood pressure and hypertension can also cause further kidney damage

**Exercise** – Regular exercise can help you to prevent and manage your blood pressure. 30 minutes of brisk exercise fve times a week is recommended to reduce your risk of stroke and other diseases. 

**Being overweight or obese** – Carrying too much weight, particularly around your middle, puts extra strain on your heart and other organs and increases your risk of high blood pressure and diabetes both of which increase your risk of stroke. 

**Diet** – processed foods contain higher levels of salt, fat and sugar which all contribute to high or elevated blood pressure as well as increasing your risk of obesity. Eating a healthy diet high in fruit and vegetables, wholegrains, healthy fats and low in salt can help you to prevent and manage hypertension. 

**Cholesterol** – More than half the people with elevated/ high blood pressure also have high cholesterol. Eating a diet low in saturated fats and regular exercise can help to prevent atherosclerosis. If cholesterol cannot be managed by dietary change, taking medications to lower your cholesterol, will help reduce your stroke risk. 

**Alcohol** – Regular and heavy use of alcohol is linked to high blood pressure but also heart rhythm disorders that increase your risk of stroke. Avoiding alcohol or sticking to recommended consumption of no more than 2 units of alcohol a day will reduce your risks. 

**Smoking** – Tobacco smoking temporarily increases your blood pressure and contributes to damaged arteries, high blood pressure and stroke as well as a range of other diseases.

**Stress** – Stress temporarily increases your blood pressure, if high levels of stress are experienced on a regular basis this can damage your arteries over time increasing your risk of stroke and other diseases. Stress can also contribute to behaviours such as poor diet, physical inactivity and excessive alcohol consumption which will increase your health risks.

# A. Vascular Structural Damage

**1. Endothelial Dysfunction:** 

![Endothelial Dysfunction](/assets/images/cvs/2-Endothelial-Dysfunction.png)

High BP damages vascular endothelium High blood pressure (hypertension) causes mechanical stress that damages the vascular endothelium (inner lining of blood vessels), leading to dysfunctional narrowing, inflammation, and reduced elasticity. This injury triggers atherosclerosis, reduces essential nitric oxide (NO) production, and increases cardiovascular risks such as strokes and heart attacks. 

**Decrease in Nitric Oxide (NO) Leads to Vasoconstriction:** 
A decrease in nitric oxide (↓ NO) bioavailability or production leads to vasoconstriction, as NO is a critical endothelium-derived relaxing factor that normally counteracts constriction. 

**Key Implications of Reduced Nitric Oxide:**
- **Vascular Tone:** Reduced NO leads to a loss of vasodilation, resulting in increased vascular smooth muscle tone and vasoconstriction.
- **Pathological Conditions:** Reduced NO availability is associated with hypertension, coronary vasospasm, and elevated systemic vascular resistance.
- **Reversal:** The vasoconstriction induced by low NO can often be reversed by supplying exogenous nitric oxide (NO donors).
- **Mechanism:** NO normally relaxes smooth muscle by activating soluble guanylyl cyclase (sGC) to produce cGMP; thus, low NO lowers cGMP levels, promoting calcium-mediated contraction.
- **Interaction with Constrictors:** When NO is deficient, the influence of vasoconstrictors like endothelin-1, angiotensin II, and sympathetic neurotransmitters (norepinephrine) increases. 

**Increased Permeability:**
Increased vascular permeability in the heart occurs primarily during pathological conditions such as ischemia, myocardial infarction (MI), and chronic inflammation, causing the vessel walls to become leaky to plasma proteins and fluids, leading to edema and reduced cardiac function. This process is largely driven by Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF-A) released by cardiomyocytes in response to stress. 

**Key Causes and Mechanisms**
- **Ischemia/Myocardial Infarction (MI):** Tissue damage, vessel occlusion, and poor blood flow trigger an acute increase in permeability, allowing inflammatory cell infiltration (e.g., neutrophils) and fluid accumulation in the infarcted myocardium.
- **VEGF-A Pathway:** Hypoxia-inducible factor 1-α (HIF-1α) increases VEGF-A expression during ischemia, which induces phosphorylation of tight junction proteins (e.g., zonula occludens-1, occludin), causing intercellular junctions in endothelial cells to open.
- **Inflammation and Mediators:** Inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β) and mediators like histamine and bradykinin can exacerbate vascular leakiness.
- **Endothelial Glycocalyx Damage:** Damage to the protective glycocalyx layer on endothelial cells leads to increased permeability and edema. 

**2. Atherosclerosis (Large Vessel Stroke)**

![Atherosclerosis](/assets/images/cvs/3-Atherosclerosis.png)

- **Lipid deposition in arteries :**
Lipid deposition in arteries, primarily low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, initiates atherosclerosis by accumulating within the intima (inner layer) of vessel walls. This process causes chronic inflammation, foam cell formation, and fatty streaks, which thicken and harden the artery (plaque buildup), obstructing blood flow and potentially leading to heart attacks or strokes. 

**Mechanism of Lipid Deposition**

- **Endothelial Dysfunction:** Damage to the inner lining of arteries (due to high blood pressure, smoking, or high cholesterol) increases its permeability.
- **Infiltration and Retention:** ApoB-containing lipoproteins (LDL, VLDL) enter the arterial wall, where they stick to proteoglycans and become oxidized (oxLDL).
- **Foam Cell Formation:** Immune cells (monocytes) enter the artery, become macrophages, and engulf the modified LDL. When these macrophages overindulge, they become foam cells, which form the fatty streaks that develop into plaques.
- **Extracellular Accumulation:** Beyond cells, excess lipids and cholesterol crystals build up in the extracellular matrix, strengthening the plaque. 

## Plaque Formation

Plaque formation in arteries, known as atherosclerosis, is the buildup of fats, cholesterol, calcium, and cellular waste, which thickens and narrows artery walls, reducing blood flow. Driven by inflammation and high LDL cholesterol, this progressive, often silent process can start in childhood, leading to heart attacks, stroke, or peripheral artery disease when plaques rupture and cause blood clots. 

**Key Aspects of Arterial Plaque Formation**

- **Process:** Damage to the inner artery lining allows low-density lipoprotein (LDL or "bad" cholesterol) to accumulate. White blood cells infiltrate this area to remove the cholesterol, becoming trapped and creating inflammation that forms a plaque.
- **Composition:** Plaque is not just fat; it's a mixture of cholesterol, fatty substances, cellular waste, calcium, and fibrin.
- **Progression:** Over years, these plaques harden (hardening of the arteries) and grow, restricting blood flow. They can eventually rupture, triggering a blood clot that may immediately block an artery, causing a heart attack or stroke.
- **Causes/Risk Factors:** Key factors include high cholesterol (especially LDL), high blood pressure, smoking, diabetes, obesity, and an unhealthy diet. 

**Common Locations & Consequences**

- **Coronary Arteries:** Supplies the heart; buildup leads to chest pain or heart attack.
- **Carotid Arteries:** Supplies the brain; plaque here can lead to a stroke.
- **Peripheral Arteries:** Supplies the arms and legs; causes pain and numbness.
- **Renal Arteries:** Supplies the kidneys; can result in kidney failure or high blood pressure. 


# B. Small Vessel Disease

![Small Vessel Disease](/assets/images/cvs/4-Small-Vessel-Disease.png)

**Mechanism:**

- **Lipohyalinosis** 
Lipohyalinosis is a degenerative disease of the small, penetrating arteries in the brain, often linked to chronic hypertension and aging. It causes vessel walls to thicken, narrowing the lumen and leading to ischemia, typically resulting in lacunar strokes. This process is a major cause of cerebral small vessel disease (cSVD). 

**Key Aspects of Lipohyalinosis**

- **Pathology:** The disease involves the breakdown of the vessel wall and its replacement with hyaline material (collagen and lipids), often including fibrinoid necrosis.
- **Causes/Risk Factors:** It is primarily caused by chronic hypertension (high blood pressure) and diabetes.
- **Mechanism:** It induces narrowing (occlusion) of small perforating arteries in the deep structures of the brain (e.g., basal ganglia, thalamus, pons), causing lacunar infarctions.
- **Relationship to Stroke:** Lipohyalinosis is a primary, distinct mechanism behind up to 45% of lacunar strokes, separate from atherosclerosis.
- **Symptoms:** Clinical presentation depends on the location of the stroke, but can include isolated motor or sensory deficits (e.g., contralteral hemiparesis), often without higher cortical signs like aphasia.
- **Diagnosis & Imaging:** While seen in autopsy as vessel destruction, it is often inferred in clinical settings via MRI showing subcortical lacunar infarcts, which are commonly 3–7 mm in size.
- **Synonyms:** It is sometimes referred to as fibrinoid degeneration of the arteriole wall. 

## Fibrinoid Necrosis 

![Fibrinoid Necrosis](/assets/images/cvs/5-Fibrinoid-Necrosis.png)

Fibrinoid necrosis is a specific form of tissue damage, typically seen in small blood vessels and glomeruli, characterized by the accumulation of plasma proteins (especially fibrin) that appear as bright pink (eosinophilic) deposits on histology. It occurs when severe immune-mediated reactions or acute, extreme high blood pressure causes endothelial injury, leading to vessel wall destruction. 

**Key Aspects of Fibrinoid Necrosis**

- **Pathophysiology:** Caused by the leakage of immune complexes and plasma proteins (like fibrinogen) into the vessel wall, resulting in a "glassy" or "fibrin-like" appearance under a microscope.
- **Causes/Associations:**
    - Immune Complex Diseases: Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), polyarteritis nodosa, and other vasculitic conditions.
    - Malignant Hypertension: Severe, high blood pressure causing intense vessel wall damage.
    - Infections/Immune reactions: Can occur due to severe, acute injury to vessel walls.
- **Symptoms:** Fibrinoid necrosis itself does not cause symptoms directly but leads to serious, often fatal damage to organs like the kidneys, heart, and brain, resulting in symptoms of the underlying disease (e.g., rapid kidney failure in glomerular involvement).
- **Types/Locations:** Commonly found in arterioles, small arteries, and glomerular capillary loops. 

# C. Hemodynamic Stress

![Hemodynamic Stress](/assets/images/cvs/6-Hemodynamic-Stress.png)

**Chronic high pressure → vessel wall thickening**

Chronic high blood pressure (hypertension) acts as a mechanical stressor on the arterial system, causing a pathological structural adaptation known as vascular remodeling, which manifests as vessel wall thickening and stiffness. 
This process occurs through several mechanisms:

- **Mechanical Stress & Hypertrophy:** Constant high pressure forces arteries to expand, leading to structural remodeling of both large and small arteries. In large conducting arteries, this manifests as intimal-medial thickening, while smaller resistance arteries often show a reduced lumen-to-wall ratio.

- **Collagen/Elastin Changes:** Chronic hypertension stimulates the production of collagen and accelerates the degradation of elastin fibers, resulting in stiffer, less compliant vessels.

- **Vascular Smooth Muscle Cell (VSMC) Alterations:** The high-pressure environment causes VSMCs to switch from a contractile to a "synthetic" phenotype, promoting further remodeling, inflammation, and remodeling, which causes more thickening.

- **Endothelial Dysfunction:** The shear stress injures the endothelial lining, allowing for inflammation, fibrosis, and calcification. 
This thickening creates a vicious cycle: as arteries become stiffer and thicker, they cannot properly expand to handle blood flow, leading to increased pulse pressure and further damage. 

## Reduced Compliance 

![Reduced Compliance](/assets/images/cvs/7-Reduced-Compliance.png)

Reduced compliance during hemodynamic stress refers to a decrease in the distensibility (or an increase in stiffness) of cardiovascular structures (arteries, veins, or the heart) when subjected to high-pressure or high-volume loading, such as during exercise, heart failure, or sepsis. This condition limits the ability of the cardiovascular system to adapt to increased cardiac output, leading to elevated pressures, reduced efficiency, and potential organ damage

### Clinical Significance
- **Heart Failure (HFpEF):** Reduced arterial and left ventricular compliance during exercise acts as a key mechanism limiting diastolic reserve and increasing filling pressures in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF).
- **Reduced Cardiac Reserve:** Reduced compliance forces the heart to work harder (higher afterload) for the same cardiac output.
- **Organ Damage:** In severe cases, this contributes to pulmonary vascular disease (via pulmonary edema) and renal dysfunction due to sodium and water retention. 


# D. Oxidative Stress & Inflammation

![Oxidative Stress and Inflammation](/assets/images/cvs/8-Oxidative-Stress-Inflammation.png)

**Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) Increase:** 
Increased Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) occur when production exceeds antioxidant defenses, leading to oxidative stress. This condition causes damage to cellular structures, including DNA, proteins, and lipids, contributing to aging and diseases like cancer and neurodegeneration. Key sources include mitochondrial dysfunction, inflammation, and environmental factors like UV light. 

### Key Aspects of Increased ROS:
- **Definition & Causes:** ROS are oxygen-containing, highly reactive molecules (e.g., superoxide, hydrogen peroxide) created by cellular metabolism and environmental stressors like pollution, tobacco smoke, and radiation.
- **Impact on Cells (Oxidative Stress):** When ROS levels surpass the cellular antioxidant capacity, it causes significant damage (oxidative stress), including membrane lipid peroxidation and damage to nucleic acids.
- **Health Implications:** Elevated ROS are linked to cardiovascular diseases, neurodegenerative disorders (Alzheimer’s, Parkinson's), cancer, and the aging process.
- **Signaling and Defense:** While excessive ROS are damaging, low-to-moderate levels are essential signaling molecules for cell proliferation and survival. The body utilizes antioxidant enzymes (e.g., SOD, catalase) to neutralize them. 

### Chronic Inflammation
Chronic inflammation is a prolonged, low-grade immune response where the body sends inflammatory cells to tissues for months or years, even without an active threat. Unlike acute, healing inflammation, this persistent state damages healthy tissues and is linked to major diseases like diabetes, cardiovascular disease, arthritis, and cancer. Common symptoms include fatigue, body pain, gastrointestinal issues, and weight changes. 

Key Aspects of Chronic Inflammation:
- **Causes:** Unresolved infections, long-term exposure to irritants (industrial chemicals, air pollution), and untreated acute inflammation.
- **Risk Factors:** Obesity, chronic stress, smoking, physical inactivity, and poor diet (high sugar/trans fats).
- **Symptoms:** Persistent fatigue, joint pain, abdominal pain, fever, skin rashes, and "brain fog".
- **Long-term Effects:** It can cause DNA damage, leading to cancer, or destroy tissue, leading to diseases like rheumatoid arthritis or Alzheimer’s.
- **Treatment & Management:** Focuses on lifestyle changes—an anti-inflammatory diet (fruits, vegetables, omega-3s), regular exercise, weight management, and stress reduction. Medication may be prescribed depending on the underlying cause. 


# E. Microvascular Damage

![Microvascular Damage](/assets/images/cvs/9-Microvascular-Damage.png)

### Arteriolar Narrowing
Arteriolar narrowing is a reduction in the caliber of small arteries, most commonly in the retina, indicating chronic, high blood pressure (hypertension) or microvascular disease. It often acts as a visible marker for systemic damage, increasing risks of chronic kidney disease, severe hypertension, and stroke, particularly when the artery-to-vein ratio decreases. 

#### Key Aspects of Arteriolar Narrowing:
- **Indicator of Hypertension**: Retinal arteriolar narrowing is a key indicator of severe or chronic hypertension, reflecting the vascular damage occurring elsewhere in the body.
- **Retinal Classification**: In Hypertensive Retinopathy, this narrowing is categorized into mild (generalized/focal), moderate, or severe (associated with swelling of the optic disc).
- **Measurement**: The condition is assessed by a decreased ratio of artery-to-vein diameter (AVR), with severe cases showing a ratio as low as 1:3 compared to a normal 2:3.
- **Distinction from Atherosclerosis**: While it is often confused with atherosclerosis (plaque buildup), arteriolar narrowing is generally associated with inflammation and high blood pressure, making it a distinct pathological process.
- **Associated Conditions**: It is closely linked to a higher likelihood of kidney failure, vascular dysfunction, and a three-fold increased risk of chronic kidney disease. 

### Reduced Blood Flow
 Reduced blood flow in microvascular damage, often termed microvascular dysfunction or disease, occurs when tiny vessels that deliver oxygen to tissues (heart, brain, kidneys) spasm, stiffen, or narrow. This dysfunction, driven by factors like high blood pressure, diabetes, and smoking, reduces tissue perfusion, causing chronic damage, ischemic pain, and organ dysfunction. 

#### Core Mechanisms of Reduced blood flow
- **Reduced Perfusion & Ischemia**: Microvascular dysfunction impairs the ability of small arteries to relax or dilate, reducing oxygen delivery to tissues.
- **Vascular Spasms & Narrowing**: Small vessels can develop structural changes or spasms, restricting blood flow and causing tissue ischemia.
- **Structural Damage**: High pressure or glucose can cause thickening of the vessel basement membrane, increasing capillary permeability.
- **Capillary Rarefaction**: A decrease in the density of functional capillaries disrupts the spatial pattern of blood flow, leading to localized ischemia.
- **Endothelial Dysfunction**: The inner lining of vessels becomes damaged, leading to inflammatory processes and increased coagulation. 


# F. Direct Cause of Hemorrhagic Stroke

![Direct Cause of Hemorrhagic Stroke](/assets/images/cvs/10-Hemorrhagic-Stroke.png)

### Persistent HTN → vessel rupture :
Persistent hypertension (high blood pressure) causes vessel rupture by creating chronic, excessive mechanical stress on artery walls, leading to weakening, remodeling, and the formation of aneurysms. 

### Mechanism of Action
- **Endothelial Damage**: Constant high pressure shears the inner lining of vessels (endothelium), causing microscopic tears.
- **Weakening & Aneurysms**: To repair this damage, the body triggers inflammation, which can weaken the vessel wall and cause it to bulge outward, forming an aneurysm.
- **Vessel Remodeling**: Persistent high pressure forces vessels to become stiffer and less elastic, reducing their ability to handle fluctuations in blood pressure.
- **Rupture**: Over time, these weakened, stiffened spots are unable to withstand the pressure and rupture, leading to dangerous internal bleeding.


# Hypertension Management in Stroke
Blood pressure (BP) management in stroke focuses on maintaining cerebral perfusion while avoiding damage, with distinct protocols for ischemia and hemorrhage. For acute ischemic stroke, maintain BP <185/110 mmHg before thrombolysis and <180/105 mmHg after. For intracerebral hemorrhage, rapidly lower systolic BP (SBP) to $\approx$140 mmHg. Chronic management aims for <130/80 mmHg. 

## Acute Ischemic Stroke (AIS) Management

- **Thrombolysis Candidates (IV t-PA)**
1. Maintain BP <185/110 mmHg before treatment and
2. <180/105 mmHg during/after for 24 hours.

- **Mechanical Thrombectomy (MT)**
1. Keep BP $\le$185/110 mmHg before 
2. $\le$180/105 mmHg 24 hours post-procedure.

- **Non-thrombolysis Candidates**: Allow "permissive hypertension" up to 220/120 mmHg for the first 24–48 hours unless treating comorbid conditions like aortic dissection or heart failure.

- **Stabilization**: If BP lowers to <220/120 mmHg, treat only if it exceeds this threshold, lowering by 15% in the first 24 hours. 

### Acute Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH) Management
- **Rapid Lowering**: If SBP is between 150 and 220 mmHg, rapidly reduce SBP to $\approx$140 mmHg (within 1–6 hours) to prevent hematoma expansion.
- **Avoidance**: Avoid excessive lowering (below 110 mmHg) and high variability.
- **Agents**: Intravenous medications like nicardipine, labetalol, or clevidipine are preferred for acute reduction. 

### Chronic/Secondary Prevention Guidelines
- **Target BP**: The general target for long-term management (after the acute period) is <130/80 mmHg.
- **Lifestyle**: Lifestyle changes are crucial for prevention, including reduced salt intake and healthy diet.
- **Medication**: Thiazide diuretics, ACE inhibitors, and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are recommended for secondary prevention. 


### Special Considerations
- **Hypotension**: Avoid hypotension to prevent reducing perfusion to the penumbra (the at-risk brain tissue).
- **Dysphagia**: Withhold oral anti-hypertensives in patients with swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) until evaluated.
